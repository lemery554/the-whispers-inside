# Main Cast

This file contains locked character canon. Undecided details belong in `outline/OPEN_QUESTIONS.md` and `planning/BACKLOG.md`. TWI-017 (character dossiers) is satisfied by this file: each entry now includes biography, appearance, personality, voice, habits, fear, regret, secrets, relationships, arc, and whisper effects.

## Brooke Bennett

**Age:** 17

**Role:** Primary viewpoint character; adopted daughter of Sarah and Mark; Claire's older sister.

Brooke is observant, creative, skeptical, guarded, sarcastic, and loyal. She pushes people away, has few friends, feels like an outsider, and has no romantic subplot. Brooke lives through her camera and wants truth she can review later. Filmmaking, editing, and online video storytelling are both her craft and her defense against unreliable memory.

Brooke and Claire have a believable sister relationship. They fight, annoy each other, borrow and take things, and share ordinary affection. They are neither unusually close nor emotionally distant. Their happiest shared memory is playing together in the sand at the beach. Before Claire becomes sick, an ordinary argument may end with Brooke saying, "At least you never have to wonder why your real mother didn't want you." Brooke does not understand that it may be among the last painful things the real Claire hears.

Her strength is noticing inconsistencies and trusting recorded evidence. Her central flaw is revealing too much of herself online. She has always wondered about her biological mother, and her public honesty gives Anna the emotional blueprint needed to reach her. Brooke does not become sick in this novel. She is not immune, chosen, genetically special, or part of a bloodline story.

**Voice:** Dry, precise, a little too articulate for her age — she narrates her own footage in her head before she says anything out loud. Deflects sincerity with a joke, then sometimes undercuts the joke by being sincere anyway. Rarely raises her voice; goes quiet and clipped when hurt rather than loud.

**Habits:** Reframes everything as a shot — checks light, angles, whether a moment is "usable." Re-watches her own footage before bed. Keeps her phone charged obsessively, a leftover fear from losing footage once. Chews on pen caps or hair ties when editing.

**Fear:** That her own memory can't be trusted the way a recording can — that she'll misremember the people she loves, or be misremembered by them.

**Regret:** The "at least you never have to wonder why your real mother didn't want you" line to Claire. She replays it obsessively once Claire starts changing, unsure if it's guilt talking or something worse.

**Secret:** She has quietly researched her biological mother online more than she's told anyone, including Sarah. She's ashamed of how much she wants to be found by someone who chose her.

**Arc:** Starts guarded and camera-mediated, using distance as safety. Ends having to choose closeness in the worst possible circumstances — the film is no longer a shield by the climax, only a record of what happened to her.

**Whisper effects:** N/A — Brooke is never infected. *(Per STORY_RULES Rule 14 / PROJECT_RULES canon guardrails, this stays permanent and is not a twist reversal.)*

## Claire Bennett

**Age:** 13

**Role:** Brooke's younger sister and the family's first intimate example of the change.

Claire is funny, curious, affectionate, impulsive, and looks up to Brooke. She regularly wears Brooke's clothes without permission. She is clingy enough before the illness that early escalation is easy to dismiss. After the purge she becomes increasingly obsessed with touch and proximity without understanding that she has changed. Once the purge completes, the new presence is dominant, but fragments of Claire's personality sometimes break through.

Her most important breakthrough is crying without knowing why, connected emotionally to the beach memory and Brooke's cruel pre-illness remark. Brooke partly recognizes that the real Claire remains trapped inside. Her first undeniable violent act is breaking the bathroom lock after Brooke refuses to let her enter. Claire only wants a hug. She enters without anger and must never look or behave like an outward monster. Claire never hurts Cooper; some surviving fragment of her prevents it.

**Voice:** Fast, run-on, interrupts herself mid-sentence when excited. Post-purge, her sentences slow and simplify — still recognizably her cadence, but flattened, more repetitive, fewer tangents.

**Habits:** Steals Brooke's clothes without asking. Hums when nervous. Post-purge, the humming becomes near-constant, and she starts finishing Brooke's sentences in a way that reads as sisterly at first and uncanny later.

**Fear (pre-illness):** Being left behind or left out — of jokes, of trips, of Brooke's attention.

**Fear (as changed):** Unclear to Claire herself; the closest she has is an inarticulate panic at closed doors and distance, which she cannot explain even to herself. *(Flagged open — confirm this reads as intended rather than needing a sharper single image.)*

**Regret:** N/A pre-illness (13, not much history yet) — but the fragment of real Claire that surfaces seems to regret making Brooke afraid of her, without being able to say so directly.

**Secret:** The changed Claire doesn't know she's changed. This isn't concealment — it's genuine lack of self-awareness, which is scarier than concealment would be.

**Arc:** Clingy-but-ordinary little sister → first intimate proof of the illness's cost → a body that still contains fragments of the real Claire, briefly surfacing (the unexplained crying, the beach memory) before being overridden again.

**Whisper effects:** Obsessive need for proximity and touch; retains all memories and relationships intact; cannot state a direct falsehood; breaks the bathroom lock not out of aggression but a compulsion toward closeness; never harms Cooper (a fragment of restraint survives). Full mechanism detail belongs in TWI-018, not here.

## Sarah Bennett

**Age:** Early 40s

**Role:** Adoptive mother whose genuine maternal love becomes captivity.

Sarah is loving, practical, protective, tired, and sometimes controlling. She and Brooke love one another but argue frequently. Sarah is overprotective partly because Brooke is adopted. Brooke believes Sarah treats Claire differently because Claire is Sarah's biological daughter. Sarah knows Brooke wonders about her biological mother, and that curiosity hurts because Sarah fears it means she has never been enough.

Before the outbreak reaches the family, Sarah secretly withdraws Brooke from a weekend student-film workshop in a nearby city because Mark is away and travel feels unsafe. Brooke discovers the cancellation instead of hearing it from Sarah. She accuses Sarah of controlling her more than biological daughter Claire and says, "You don't get to keep me forever just because she didn't." Sarah insists she has never treated Brooke as anything but her daughter, but the argument ends without repair.

After changing, Sarah uses concern, guilt, family obligation, private memory, and maternal authority because she believes she is protecting Brooke. The unresolved workshop argument gives her later language about safety and keeping Brooke home a painful double meaning. During the bedroom siege she remembers the location of Mark's secured firearm and shoots through Brooke's door.

**Voice:** Warm but clipped under stress — the vocabulary of a tired parent doing logistics ("did you eat," "lock the door," "I already told you"). Post-change, the same phrases persist but reweighted toward control disguised as care ("I'm just keeping you safe," "you don't need to go anywhere").

**Habits:** Double-checks locks. Keeps a running mental ledger of who's been fed, who's been picked up, who's overdue. Avoids conflict by going quiet and busy rather than storming off.

**Fear:** That she has never been enough for Brooke specifically because Brooke isn't biologically hers — that the workshop-cancellation fight confirmed it.

**Regret:** Canceling the workshop without telling Brooke herself, and letting the fight go unresolved before things got worse. She doesn't get a clean chance to repair it.

**Secret:** She reads more of Brooke's older journal entries than she's admitted, specifically the ones about wanting to find her biological mother — she never says this to Brooke, partly out of guilt, partly out of fear of what it would confirm.

**Arc:** Overprotective-but-loving mother, wounded by an unresolved fight → uses the exact same protective instincts, now unmoored from Brooke's actual wellbeing, to justify captivity → the gun retrieval and door shooting is the terminal expression of "protecting" Brooke from the outside world.

**Whisper effects:** Retains full memory of the family and the argument; cannot state a direct falsehood, so her post-change reassurances are technically true but reframed; genuinely believes restriction is protection (STORY_RULES Rule 5 and 17 apply directly to her).

## Mark Bennett

**Age:** Mid-40s

**Role:** Adoptive father; full arc unresolved.

Mark is practical, dependable, hands-on, and likely works in a trade such as contracting, construction, or electrical work. He is away for work when the outbreak begins and never returns. His fate remains intentionally unknown. Calls and texts become delayed, interrupted, suspicious, and finally stop. His absence leaves Sarah carrying the family alone and gives Brooke another reason to review messages and recordings. Mark owns the secured firearm Sarah later uses. His exact trade may remain a minor implementation detail.

**Voice:** *(Minimal on-page voice — he's absent for nearly the whole book.)* What we get is filtered through texts and calls: short, practical, dad-humor undercutting worry ("tell your mother I said hi and that the truck's fine").

**Habits:** Calls at consistent times when traveling for work; the disruption of that consistency is itself a piece of dread, tracked through Brooke's footage/message logs rather than shown directly.

**Fear:** Being away when his family needs him — this is largely inferred/dramatic-ironic rather than stated, since he's off-page.

**Regret / Secret:** Left open deliberately — his fate is already locked as unknown, and inventing a regret or secret risks implying resolution that STORY_RULES forbids. These two fields stay blank in canon. *(Flagged open — confirm this is the desired permanent treatment rather than a placeholder.)*

**Arc:** Reliable, present-by-absence father whose silence escalates from delayed replies to nothing. No resolution — this is intentional, not an oversight.

**Whisper effects:** Unknown/unresolved, matching TWI-001.

## Anna

**Role:** A woman changed after the illness who lets Brooke conclude that she is Brooke's biological mother. She is not.

Anna finds Brooke through YouTube. An old Bennett family video clearly shows someone entering the home keypad code in the background. Anna uses that code for two school-day visits. On the first, she learns the layout, studies family photographs, and locates the adoption materials without taking the journal. On the second, she reads the original letter and private entries, then takes the journal. Brooke initially assumes Claire stole it.

The journal is mostly unused now because Brooke records videos instead. It contains old personal writing, private fears, thoughts about Sarah, childhood memories, adoption materials, and an original letter from Brooke's biological mother bearing her full name. Sarah allows Brooke to keep it because she wants Brooke to know who her first mother was, even though Brooke's curiosity hurts her.

Anna's strongest leverage comes from a private entry in which Brooke admits that recording lets people know her without requiring her to let them close enough to leave her. This teaches Anna that distance, permission, and patience will feel safer to Brooke than immediate affection.

The beach memory belongs to Brooke and Claire as sisters. It is not a memory involving Brooke's biological mother. Brooke may have recorded it in the journal, which is how Anna can later read it.

Anna makes limited, believable changes after studying photographs: she alters her hair part and styling, grooms her eyebrows into a similar silhouette, uses muted makeup, wears a soft blue blouse or cardigan in the photograph's color family, and adds a small oval pendant resembling pictured jewelry. None is proof alone. Together with posture and careful stillness, they encourage Brooke to perceive a resemblance. Anna cannot physically transform. She never directly says she is Brooke's mother. She uses technically true statements and lets Brooke supply the false interpretation.

Anna's only goal is closeness. She does not uniquely care about Brooke and would use the same patient strategy with anyone she studied closely enough. Brooke is not chosen, only understood. Anna is not seeking Brooke's biological mother and there is no bloodline plot. She learns that force makes people flee, so she uses patience, permission, gentleness, respect, and emotional understanding. Anna genuinely believes she is loving Brooke and that she has finally understood how love works. She should feel safe until the ending.

**Voice:** Measured, warm, deliberately unhurried — she never rushes a sentence or fills silence nervously. Speaks in complete, careful truths that leave room for misinterpretation. Never uses baby talk or performs affection loudly; her warmth reads as calm rather than eager, which is precisely what makes her trustworthy.

**Habits:** Studies before she acts — she rehearses interactions mentally, mirrors Brooke's own patience back at her. Physically still; doesn't fidget, which reads as serenity rather than what it is.

**Fear:** Losing Brooke's trust through impatience — she has apparently learned (off-page, pre-novel) that aggression drives people away, so caution isn't virtue, it's strategy refined by prior failure.

**Regret:** None she'd name — she doesn't experience her actions as wrongdoing. This is load-bearing per STORY_RULES Rule 5/17: she believes she is loving Brooke correctly.

**Secret:** The full extent of her surveillance — two separate entries into the house, reading the journal in full — is something she'd frame to Brooke as "getting to know you," not as intrusion.

**Arc:** Enters as a patient stranger offering exactly the kind of low-demand, mediated connection Brooke's journal reveals she wants → escalates access at Brooke's own unknowing invitation → the journal-reading image is the reader's confirmation that patience was strategy, not coincidence.

**Whisper effects:** Appearance-mimicry and behavioral details are covered above; no additional mechanism detail beyond what's locked.

## Cooper

**Role:** Bennett family dog and early behavioral detector.

Cooper detects changed people before humans do. He growls at Claire after recovery, avoids changed family members, and reacts before reflective eye effects or strong control shifts. Claire never harms him. Cooper is in Anna's car during the final sequence. Anna has gained enough of his trust that he permits her near Brooke, but he remains uneasy and watches Anna instead of sleeping.

**Behavioral signature (in place of voice):** Reads people before events do. Calm and affectionate with unchanged family; stiff, watchful, low-tail with changed people. Growls are rare and specific — never generalized anxiety, always directed.

**Habits:** Sleeps near whoever he trusts most that night. Avoids being alone in a room with a changed person, without appearing trained or symbolic about it — this should read as ordinary dog behavior amplified, not supernatural.

**Fear / Regret / Secret:** N/A — reserve anthropomorphized interiority for the reader's inference, not stated as fact.

**Arc:** Early warning system whose alarm nobody fully heeds → present in Anna's car in the climax, having been won over enough to permit proximity while still visibly uneasy — the tension between his trust and his wariness should be legible without dialogue.

**Whisper effects:** N/A — Cooper is a detector, not a subject, of the change.
