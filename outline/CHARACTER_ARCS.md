# Character Arcs — TWI-101

Maps each character's starting belief, the pressure applied across the month, their choices, reversals, lowest point, climax behavior, and ending state. Built from locked canon in `bible/characters/MAIN_CAST.md` and `bible/infection/RULES.md`/`EVOLUTION.md`. Nothing here overrides those files; where a beat isn't yet locked, it's flagged for `outline/OPEN_QUESTIONS.md` rather than decided here.

## Brooke Bennett

**Starting belief:** Distance is safety. Recording a moment lets her keep the truth of it without having to risk the messiness of being fully present or fully known by anyone.

**Pressure:** Claire changes first, inside her own home. The illness makes closeness itself the threat, which directly attacks the belief that distance protects her — now the people closest to her are the danger, and the camera can't keep her safe from her own family.

**Choices:** Keeps filming even as it becomes dangerous, both because it's how she processes fear and because the audience becomes a lifeline for other frightened people. Chooses to keep investigating (the keypad footage, the missing journal) rather than looking away. Repeatedly chooses proximity to family members she knows are changing, out of love, against her own instinct for self-preservation.

**Reversals:** The unresolved fight with Sarah about the workshop reframes Sarah's later "protective" language with a second, darker meaning. The journal theft, first blamed on Claire, redirects suspicion in a way that delays Brooke's realization about Anna. The moment Brooke starts to want Anna's calm, undemanding presence is itself a reversal — the girl who trusts distance starts to crave a relationship built entirely on distance, without recognizing the danger in that appeal.

**Lowest point:** Isolated from both her mother and sister, unable to trust the two people she's supposed to be safest with, with her father unreachable and no adult she can rely on — she has never been more alone, and the loneliness itself is what makes Anna's patient attention land.

**Climax behavior:** Brooke initiates the closeness — per STORY_RULES Rule 15, Anna wins through consent, not force. Brooke's own established coping mechanism (trusting curated, mediated connection over messy immediate intimacy) is exactly what makes her choose to trust Anna at the worst possible moment.

**Ending state:** Alive, but the final journal-reading image confirms to the reader (not necessarily fully to Brooke) that patience was strategy. Brooke's arc doesn't resolve into safety — it resolves into the same isolation that created her vulnerability in the first place, now confirmed rather than suspected.

## Claire Bennett

**Starting belief:** *(Too young/undeveloped for a stated worldview — her "belief" is more instinctive: closeness with Brooke is safety and belonging.)*

**Pressure:** The purge and subsequent change hijack that instinct for closeness and turn it compulsive and boundary-blind, without Claire's awareness that anything has happened to her.

**Choices:** Claire doesn't make choices in the traditional arc sense post-change — the story's horror is precisely that her agency is gone while her affect remains. Pre-change, her choices are ordinary sisterly ones (borrowing clothes, wanting in on things).

**Reversals:** The bathroom door-breaking is read by Brooke first as aggression, then recontextualized — Claire wanted only a hug. The unexplained crying is a reversal in the other direction: proof a fragment of the real Claire is still in there, right when Brooke (and the reader) has started to accept she's gone.

**Lowest point:** The completed change, when the "new presence is dominant" per MAIN_CAST.md — Claire is present in body, voice, and memory, but no longer recognizably making her own choices.

**Climax behavior:** Present in the final sequence as part of the changed collective; her restraint regarding Cooper is the one clear proof, right up to the end, that some fragment of her original self persists.

**Ending state:** Changed, memory intact, physically safe and "recovered" by every visible metric — the horror is that this is presented, and in-world accepted, as a good outcome.

## Sarah Bennett

**Starting belief:** Being a good mother means keeping her daughters safe, even if that requires decisions they resent her for (the workshop cancellation).

**Pressure:** Mark's disappearance leaves her solely responsible; Claire's change forces her to decide how to react to a daughter who looks recovered but isn't behaving like herself; the unresolved fight with Brooke means she's carrying guilt with no way to repair it before things escalate further.

**Choices:** Chooses not to tell Brooke the real reason for canceling the workshop until it's already too late to have the conversation calmly. Once changed, chooses to use every protective instinct she has — but now unmoored from what Brooke actually needs.

**Reversals:** The same protectiveness that estranged her from Brooke before the illness becomes, after her change, the literal captivity that endangers Brooke. Her guilt over the unresolved fight becomes fuel for language about safety and "keeping her home" that now carries a second, sinister meaning neither of them chose.

**Lowest point:** Retrieving Mark's firearm and shooting through Brooke's own bedroom door — the terminal expression of protection turned into violence against the person she's trying to protect.

**Climax behavior:** Acts entirely within the "changed" behavioral rules (retains memory, cannot lie, believes she's protecting Brooke) — her violence is sincere, not malicious in her own understanding.

**Ending state:** Changed, in the house, presumably still present in Brooke's life in some capacity going forward — full resolution of Sarah's specific fate should be confirmed against the ending chapters once TWI-107 (ending fairness audit) is run.

## Anna

**Starting belief:** *(Pre-novel, off-page)* Force and pursuit fail — she has apparently already learned this the hard way before the story begins, which is why patience isn't presented as her innate personality but as strategy refined by prior failure.

**Pressure:** None, really — Anna is the only major character under no external pressure. This asymmetry is part of what makes her dangerous: she's operating on her own timeline, unhurried, while everyone else is reacting to crisis.

**Choices:** Studies Brooke's public videos and private journal before making contact. Makes two separate school-day entries into the house rather than one, escalating access deliberately. Chooses appearance changes calculated to suggest resemblance without claiming it outright. Never forces a claim of motherhood — lets Brooke supply the conclusion herself, in line with STORY_RULES Rule 10.

**Reversals:** From the reader's perspective, Anna reverses from "patient stranger offering safety" to "the person who was studying Brooke the whole time" — but this isn't a reversal in Anna's own understanding, since she never experiences herself as deceptive.

**Lowest point:** N/A in the traditional sense — Anna doesn't have a low point built into her arc; she is the one character whose trajectory is uninterrupted escalation toward success.

**Climax behavior:** Present in the car in the final sequence with Cooper's uneasy tolerance; the journal-reading image is her confirming action, proving to the reader what her patience was for.

**Ending state:** In proximity to Brooke, having "won" per STORY_RULES Rule 15 — Brooke chose the closeness. Anna's ending state is success, which is the specific horror STORY_RULES Rule 18 requires: this could have been anyone; Brooke was understood, not chosen.

## Cooper

**Starting belief:** N/A — reserve interiority for reader inference only, per dossier notes.

**Pressure:** Repeated exposure to changed family members and, eventually, Anna, without the ability to communicate what he detects to anyone who could act on it.

**Choices:** Consistently avoids and grows wary around changed people; consistently permits and stays close to unchanged people he trusts.

**Reversals:** Gradual, grudging tolerance of Anna specifically — not trust so much as an absence of alarm, won through Anna's patience the same way she wins Brooke's trust, which is its own quiet horror: even the detector can be worn down.

**Lowest point:** N/A — Cooper doesn't have an emotional low point on the page; his function is signal, not journey.

**Climax behavior:** In Anna's car during the final sequence — present, watchful, uneasy, but not objecting.

**Ending state:** Physically safe, still alert, still watching Anna rather than sleeping — the reader's last behavioral cue that something remains wrong even in an apparently resolved ending.
