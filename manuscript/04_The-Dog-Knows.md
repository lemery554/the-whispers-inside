# Chapter 4: The Dog Knows

At nine Saturday morning, Brooke's phone reminded her to leave for a workshop she was no longer attending.

She dismissed the alert and stayed in bed. Downstairs, the vacuum cleaner struck the same baseboard three times while Sarah cleaned a house that was not dirty. Claire's door remained closed across the hall.

The Harlan Media Center opened registration at ten. At ten-fifteen, students would meet their production groups. At eleven, they would rotate through lighting and sound. Brooke knew because the schedule was still attached to the confirmation email, preserved alongside the cancellation she had recorded the night before.

At ten, she opened the workshop's social page. Someone had posted a photograph of name badges arranged on a table. The nearest badge belonged to a girl named Elise. Behind it, twenty-three others waited in clean white rows.

Brooke closed the page before she could count them.

Her outbreak upload had reached eighty-six views. The fruit-punch comment had collected more likes than the video. Two new viewers said their schools had sent notices about the illness. One wrote that her father had recovered overnight and was already back at work.

Brooke saved screenshots, then hated herself for feeling relieved by the small purpose they gave her.

A soft scrape came from the hall. Cooper stood outside Claire's bedroom with his nose near the gap beneath the door. He did not scratch to be let in. His tail hung low, and the fur between his shoulders moved when he breathed.

"What are you doing?"

One ear turned toward Brooke. The rest of him remained aimed at the door.

Claire called from inside, "If that's Brooke, I'm dead."

"It's the police. We found several stolen garments."

Silence followed. Yesterday, Claire would have answered before Brooke finished the sentence.

Brooke crouched beside Cooper. "Come on."

He backed away at once, so quickly his nails clicked against the hardwood. He stopped beside Brooke's knee and looked at the door.

"Cooper?" Claire called. His ears flattened. Brooke knocked. "Open up."

"I'm sleeping."

"You're talking."

"Sleep talking. It's serious."

The joke was weak, but it was an offer. Brooke could answer it, reopen the familiar rhythm, and perhaps find a way toward the apology she had refused in the hallway.

"Mom wants your laundry," Brooke said instead.

"No, she doesn't."

"She might eventually."

"Go away."

Brooke stood. Cooper followed her into her room without being asked. When she tried to send him back into the hall, he settled beneath her desk and placed his chin over both front paws.

At noon, Sarah made grilled cheese and tomato soup. Claire came down wearing loose pajama pants and her own gray sweatshirt. She said she was tired. Sarah touched the back of her hand to Claire's forehead and announced there was no fever.

"I'm not sick," Claire said.

"I didn't say you were."

"You did the mom thermometer."

Sarah set a bowl in front of her. "Eat something."

Claire tore the sandwich in half but did not lift it. Cooper lay beside Brooke's chair, farther from Claire than usual. When Claire held a corner of crust beneath the table, he turned his head away.

"Traitor," Claire said.

Normally Cooper accepted bread as if famine had been narrowly avoided. Brooke watched him press closer to her ankle.

"Maybe he already ate," Sarah said.

"He has never already eaten," Brooke replied.

Claire dropped the crust onto her plate. "He knows you're mad at me."

"Dogs don't understand grudges."

"Then why does he like you?"

The answer was close enough to normal that Sarah smiled. Brooke did too before she could prevent it. Claire looked between them and gave a small, satisfied nod.

For several minutes they ate without mentioning the workshop or yesterday's fight. Rain began against the kitchen windows, light enough to sound like fingers brushing the glass. The television showed footage of a temporary intake tent outside a hospital in Hartford. A reporter stood beneath an umbrella and said officials were investigating whether the gastrointestinal illness was foodborne, viral, or the result of an environmental exposure.

"Every option," Brooke said.

Sarah reached for the remote. "They don't know yet."

"That's what I said."

"No. You said it like not knowing is evidence they're hiding something."

"I didn't say anybody was hiding anything."

Claire pushed her bowl away. "Can you both not?"

Sarah muted the television. "How much did you eat?"

"Enough."

The sandwich halves remained intact. Claire had taken three spoonfuls of soup, leaving pale swirls where the cheese had touched the surface.

"Your stomach hurt?" Sarah asked.

"No."

"Nauseous?"

"I'm tired."

Sarah performed the hand thermometer again. This time Claire ducked away.

"Stop."

"You're warm."

"Your hands are cold."

Both could be true. Brooke looked down at Cooper. He had raised his head.

Claire carried her bowl to the sink and left it on the counter. Cooper watched her cross the kitchen. When she passed behind Brooke, his body tightened against her chair.

Brooke felt the movement through the wood.

"Cooper," she said quietly.

Claire stopped. "What?"

"Nothing."

Cooper stood and moved to the other side of Brooke's legs.

Sarah noticed. Her eyes narrowed briefly, then moved to the untouched sandwich. "Go lie down. I'll bring you water."

"I'm not sick."

"I know. Go rest anyway."

Claire left without arguing. Halfway to the stairs, she pressed one hand flat against her abdomen.

Brooke saw it. Sarah saw Brooke see it.

"It's probably nothing," Sarah said. Brooke did not answer.

Upstairs, Claire put on one of Brooke's old camera tests without asking. Brooke heard Cooper barking through the wall, then her own voice telling somebody to hold the reflector still. The clip restarted twice. On the third pass, Brooke went across the hall.

Claire lay on top of the covers with Brooke's laptop balanced against her knees. "You said I could borrow this."

"In 2022."

"No expiration date."

The image on the screen had paused on Claire in the backyard, squinting into reflected sunlight while Cooper tried to drag the collapsible screen away by one handle. Brooke sat at the foot of the bed. "Why are you watching that?"

"I wanted to see if you were always bossy."

"What did the evidence show?"

"You used to let me help."

Brooke could still apologize for yesterday. She could say the sentence had been designed to hurt and therefore said more about her than Claire. Instead she reached for the laptop. "You're getting fingerprints on it."

Claire closed the screen before Brooke took it. "I'm sorry about the workshop."

"You said Mom was right."

"I can be sorry and think she's right."

The answer was irritatingly reasonable. Brooke looked toward the doorway, where Cooper stood with his front feet in the hall and his head extended into the room. Claire patted the mattress.

"Come here, Coop." He shifted his weight backward.

Claire frowned. "Do I smell?"

"More than usual."

She threw a pillow. It fell short of Brooke and landed near the door. Cooper startled away. When Brooke retrieved it, she felt his body trembling against her leg.

"He's being weird," Claire said.

"He was weird before you were born."

Brooke guided him into the hall and shut Claire's door partway, leaving the gap she always demanded. The camera test began again behind it. Cooper followed Brooke to her room, still glancing back.

By three, Claire had developed a fever of 101.4. Sarah took her temperature twice with the digital thermometer and once with an older one from the bathroom cabinet because she distrusted the first number. Claire complained that the beeping hurt her head. The curtains in her room were closed, though the rain had already darkened the afternoon.

Brooke stood in the doorway with her phone in her hand. She had begun recording when Sarah read the second temperature aloud. The camera held the clock, the thermometer, and Claire curled beneath her blanket.

"Turn that off," Claire said.

"I'm keeping track."

"Use paper."

"Paper doesn't have a clock."

"Clocks have clocks."

Sarah looked over her shoulder. "Brooke."

Brooke stopped recording but did not put the phone away.

Claire shifted onto her back. Sweat had darkened her hairline. "Can you get Mom?"

"She's right there."

Claire looked toward Sarah as though surprised to find her beside the bed. Confusion crossed her face and vanished. "I know. I meant water."

Sarah picked up the glass from the nightstand. "Small sips."

Claire drank, winced, and pressed her lips together. Sarah took the glass before it spilled.

In the hall, Cooper paced from Brooke's room to the top stair and back. He would not cross Claire's doorway.

Brooke filmed twelve seconds of him privately, including the time on her screen. When Cooper noticed the phone, he looked past it toward Claire's room.

At five, Mark texted that the crew had found damage behind a wall and he would call when he knew whether they could leave. Sarah typed a response with both thumbs, erased it, and called him. The call went to voicemail.

"His phone dies in that building," she said. Brooke had not asked.

Claire slept for an hour. When she woke, the fever had reached 102.6. She refused soup, crackers, applesauce, and the lemon ice pops she normally ate until her tongue hurt.

"The lemon ones taste wrong," she said.

"They're the same box," Sarah told her.

"They're bad."

Sarah opened a new one and tasted it herself. "It tastes normal."

Claire turned her face into the pillow.

At seven-thirteen, she came out of her room without socks and stood at the top of the stairs. Brooke was below her, carrying clean towels from the dryer. Cooper rested on the landing between them. He woke before Claire spoke.

His head rose. His body went rigid. A sound gathered low in his chest, not yet the growl from the night of the hospital footage but close enough that Brooke stopped moving.

Claire gripped the banister. "My stomach hurts."

Cooper backed down one step. Brooke dropped the towels. She reached Claire as her knees folded, catching her beneath the arms before her head struck the railing. The phone in Brooke's pocket pressed against Claire's hip. For once, Brooke did not think about taking it out.

Claire's skin burned through the sweatshirt. She made a small sound and clutched Brooke's hand with both of hers.

"Don't go," she whispered.

"I'm not going anywhere."

Sarah ran from the kitchen. Cooper remained two steps below, trembling as he watched them.

Claire bent forward sharply. Brooke barely turned her in time. Vomit struck the scattered towels and spread across the landing. For one second, nobody moved. Then Sarah began giving instructions, and the house stopped being ordinary.
