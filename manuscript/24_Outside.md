# Chapter 24

## Outside

Brooke left through the front door.

The choice felt reckless after the backpack, the loosened screen, and the route mapped over the porch roof. It was also the only exit that did not announce escape.

Sarah was in the basement sorting the installer papers. Claire had fallen asleep on the couch with one hand resting on Cooper's back. The dog opened his eyes when Brooke crossed the foyer.

Brooke put one finger to her lips.

Cooper stood anyway.

She could not leave him in the house with both of them, and she could not take him into a diner without making herself easier to find. She clipped on his leash.

The keypad chirped when the door closed behind them.

Brooke waited on the porch for Sarah to call her name. Nothing happened.

She walked toward the back road instead of the town center, keeping to streets with dark yards and parked cars. Cooper moved quickly but checked behind them every few houses.

The town was not empty. A grocery store near the green remained open. People carried takeout. A bus sighed at the curb with six passengers sitting in three close pairs. Church bells marked ten o'clock as if the week had earned an ordinary Friday night.

The diner windows glowed at the corner.

Brooke stopped across the street.

Anna sat alone in a booth near the front, exactly where someone would sit after promising Brooke the door.

Her hair was different from the dog park. Darker, or perhaps only styled with a side part that made her face look narrower. She wore a soft blue cardigan and a small oval pendant. None of it should have meant anything.

Brooke had seen that shade of blue in the photograph inside the adoption folder.

Cooper stared through the glass. His tail stayed low, but he did not growl.

Anna looked up. She did not wave.

Brooke crossed the street. Before entering, she started an audio recording and put the phone in her coat pocket with the microphone exposed.

The waitress objected to Cooper until Anna asked whether the patio could be opened. The outdoor tables had been stacked for the season, but the waitress relented when Brooke said they would sit near the gate. Anna carried her own coffee outside. The cup tipped against the doorframe and coffee ran over her thumb.

"Sorry," Anna said, irritated with herself. She wiped her hand with a paper napkin and left a brown crescent on the cuff of the blue cardigan. She did not take Brooke's leash or pull out her chair.

"You came," Anna said.

"You gave me an address."

"Yes."

"That's not the same thing."

"No."

Brooke chose the chair closest to the sidewalk. Cooper sat beneath it, body angled toward Anna.

Anna looked at him. "Hello again."

His ears shifted.

"Don't touch him," Brooke said.

"I won't."

Anna folded both hands around the coffee cup.

Up close, the resemblance was less convincing and harder to dismiss. The eyebrow shape. The angle of her head when she listened. The pendant, almost like the one worn by the young woman in Sarah's photograph.

Brooke hated herself for cataloging it.

"How do you know what question I've been asking?"

"You told everyone."

"Not her name."

"No."

The answer sat between them.

"What is it?" Brooke asked.

"The name you copied into the journal."

Brooke said the full name from the bottom of the letter.

"Yes."

Brooke's hand tightened around Cooper's leash.

The street sounds receded. A car passed. Inside the diner, a server laughed too loudly at something near the counter.

"Where did you hear that?"

"From something written for you."

Brooke thought of the letter inside the locked box. She thought of the missing journal where she had copied the name and parts of the letter as a child, practicing the letters until they looked like they belonged to her.

"My journal."

Anna did not answer.

"Were you in my house?"

"You should ask every question you need to ask."

"That isn't an answer."

"No."

Brooke pushed her chair back an inch.

Anna did not move. "You can leave."

"You know my mother's name."

"Yes."

"You know what she wrote."

"I know what she wanted you to know."

"Did she send you?"

"No."

The direct denial struck harder than a comforting answer would have.

Brooke looked at Anna's face again, searching for something inherited rather than assembled. "Are you Evelyn?"

Anna's expression changed. Not guilt. Something quieter.

"You want me to say yes."

"Are you?"

"I came because of you."

Brooke heard the evasion and filled it with hope anyway.

"Why now?"

"Because now I knew where to find you."

"You watched the videos."

"Every second."

"And before that?"

Anna looked down at her coffee. "I did not understand before."

Brooke felt the words enter the empty place her biological mother had occupied for years. Regret. Shame. A woman too frightened or too broken to return until she saw the daughter she had lost speaking to strangers online.

There were other meanings. Brooke could still see them if she tried.

"You should not trust me because you want this to be true," Anna said.

It was exactly what a safe person would say.

"Sarah is my mother."

"Yes."

No jealousy. No correction.

"I love her."

"I know."

"Even now."

"Especially now."

Brooke looked away because the answer was too kind.

Anna waited.

No questions about where Brooke lived. No demand to see the letter. No request for forgiveness. She let silence remain silence instead of turning it into pressure.

"Why did you leave me?" Brooke asked.

Anna's fingers tightened slightly around the cup.

"I cannot give you the answer you deserve."

The sentence was not an explanation. Brooke heard it as one.

Cooper rose and pressed his shoulder against Brooke's shin. Anna watched him, then looked toward the diner window. A girl eating alone had glanced up from her phone and seemed to recognize Brooke.

"You should go," Anna said.

Brooke almost laughed. "You asked me here."

"And you came. You do not owe me anything else."

"Will I see you again?"

Anna's gaze returned to her. "If you choose to."

Brooke stood. She wanted Anna to stop her. The fact that Anna did not made Brooke want to stay.

"I don't know what to call you," Brooke said.

"Anna is true."

Another careful sentence.

Brooke led Cooper through the patio gate. When she looked back, Anna remained seated with both hands around the cup.

She had not asked for a hug.

At the corner, Brooke stopped the audio recording. The file showed twenty-eight minutes and saved locally beneath the neutral title her phone assigned it.

Across the street, a man beneath the pharmacy awning glanced at her, then returned to his phone. The waitress came outside carrying a bus tub and noticed Brooke standing alone with Cooper.

"Are you all right?" the waitress called.

"Fine."

"You're out late."

"My mom knows."

The words came automatically. They were also a lie.

A girl who had been eating alone came through the diner door behind the waitress. "That's Brooke Bennett," she said. "From the videos."

The waitress's concern sharpened. "The missing girl?"

"I'm not missing."

Cooper gave a low warning.

Brooke shortened the leash and kept walking.

The girl followed as far as the corner. "I can walk with you."

"No, thanks."

"You shouldn't be alone."

Brooke looked back toward the patio.

Anna was inside at the register, paying the bill. She met Brooke's eyes through the glass and did not come out.

Brooke turned onto the darker road toward home. Footsteps followed at an unhurried distance.

The girl from the diner was following her.
