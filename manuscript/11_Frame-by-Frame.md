# Chapter 11: Frame by Frame

Brooke showed her mother the wrist in the morning, and it did not go the way she had planned it in the shower.

She had planned to be calm. She had the photograph with the timestamp and the four marks still faintly there under the skin, yellowed now instead of red, and she had planned to lay it in front of Sarah like a fact instead of an accusation. Facts were harder to argue with. That was the whole reason she recorded things.

Sarah was at the counter with her coffee and her phone, reading a news alert she read aloud without being asked, because reading it aloud was a way of settling the kitchen. Recovery rates were above ninety percent statewide. The health department had updated its guidance. The word *isolate* had been quietly replaced in the new version by the phrase *support at home*, and Sarah said this as though it were good news, which, if you did not know anything, it was.

"Claire grabbed my wrist last night," Brooke said. "Hard. Look."

She held out her arm. Sarah looked. Brooke watched her mother's eyes move over the four marks and do the work of turning them into nothing, and it was like watching someone read a subtitle in a language they had decided in advance to distrust.

"She's clingy," Sarah said. "She almost died on Sunday, Brooke. She's a thirteen-year-old who spent a weekend so scared she couldn't say what room she was in, and now she wants to hold onto her big sister, and her big sister is photographing her hands like a crime scene."

"She wouldn't let go. I told her she was hurting me and she kept squeezing. I had to wait for the dog to bark before her hand opened."

"So the dog barked and she let go. That's what you're describing. She let go."

"After."

Sarah set the phone face down. She did that when she was choosing her words, and the words she chose were quiet, which was worse than shouting. "I know you're frightened. I've been frightened too, I was there in that bathroom with her. But you have decided something about your sister, and you are collecting evidence for it, and I need you to hear how that sounds. She is not a project. She is not one of your videos. She's a little girl who is finally okay, and every time you look at her you make her into something wrong."

"I'm not making her into anything. I'm writing down what happens."

"You're deciding what it means before it happens." Sarah picked the phone back up, which meant the conversation was ending. "It's unkind, Brooke. I don't have a nicer word for it. She adores you, and you're treating her like she's contagious."

The word *unkind* did what Sarah had aimed it to do. It found the place in Brooke that had already used the worst sentence it owned on Claire in a school hallway six days ago, the sentence Brooke still heard at night, and it pressed there. Maybe that was the whole thing. Maybe Brooke had said an unforgivable thing to her sister and could not bear the guilt of it, and so she had started building a story where the person she had hurt was not really her sister anymore, where the cruelty was aimed at something that deserved suspicion. Maybe the list was not evidence. Maybe it was an alibi.

She almost believed it. That was the part she would think about later. Standing in the kitchen with her wrist still out, she came within a breath of agreeing that she was the problem, because it was the kinder story and it let her mother stay in the room with her.

Then she went upstairs and did the one thing she trusted more than her own guilt, which was to look at the footage.

She had a lot of it now. She had been recording Claire for days, small pieces, the hallway and the kitchen and the camera tests Claire kept borrowing, a dozen clips she had told herself were for a doctor and had never sent to one. She loaded them onto the desktop and laid them out on the editing timeline in order, a week of her sister rendered as a strip of thumbnails, and she began at the beginning and went through them the way she went through anything she was serious about, which was slowly, one pass for picture and one for sound, not trusting either sense to catch what the other missed.

For a long time there was nothing. There was Claire eating mushrooms. There was Claire humming. There was Claire standing a little too close and following a little too readily, all of it exactly as unremarkable as Sarah said, all of it deniable. Brooke started to feel the sick tilt of being wrong. A camera did not lie, but it also did not care, and its indifference was starting to feel like a verdict against her.

She found it in a clip from two nights before.

It was an evening clip, dim, shot in the upstairs hall with only the weak night-light near the bathroom for illumination. Claire stood at the far end where the streetlight came through the landing window and striped the floor. Brooke had recorded it for no reason she could remember, twelve seconds of nothing, Claire in the half-dark looking down the stairs at something, Cooper somewhere below her refusing to come up. At normal speed it was a girl in a hallway.

Brooke slowed it to a quarter and stepped through it a frame at a time, and on the frame where Claire lifted her head toward the camera, her eyes caught the light and threw it back green.

Not the red of a phone flash on a retina. Brooke knew that color, every photographer knew it, the flat demonic red of a pupil bouncing a strobe. This was not that. There was no flash. The hall had been nearly black. And the color was not red but a cold reflective green, the green of an animal at the edge of headlights, there in one frame and gone in the next, catching a light source Brooke could not account for because there had not been enough light in the room to catch.

She sat back.

She went forward a frame. Gone. Back a frame. Gone. Only the one, only the instant Claire's head came up, her eyes holding a shine that the darkness around her should have made impossible. Brooke isolated the frame and exported it. She found two more across the week, both in low light, both a single frame each, both at moments when Claire had gone still and focused on something. In brighter clips there was nothing, no matter how slowly she looked. Whatever it was, it lived in the dark, and it showed itself to the camera more readily than it had ever shown itself to Brooke standing three feet away.

She did not know what it meant. She was careful with herself about that. She did not have a theory and she refused to invent one, because the second she invented one she would start seeing it everywhere and become exactly the person her mother had described. All she had was three still frames of her sister's eyes doing a thing eyes did not do, dated and exported and real. She put them in a folder. She named the folder with the date and nothing else.

Three frames could be a coincidence. A sensor could misread a stray reflection, a passing car, a phone screen out of shot. She knew the ways cameras invented light, and she knew that the only thing separating evidence from a ghost story was whether you could make the thing happen again on purpose. So she decided to make it happen again on purpose, and she was most of the way through deciding it before she let herself feel how ugly the plan was.

She waited until after dark. She turned off the overhead in her room and left only the monitor, angled down so it lit the desk and not the bed, and she set her phone on the shelf where it could see the whole room, and she started it recording, and then she opened her door and did not have to call. Claire was in the hall within a minute, the way she was always within a minute now, drawn to the opening door like water finding the low place in a floor.

"You're up late," Claire said, and came in, and sat in the spot she always took at the edge of the bed, in the near-dark, glad. "I couldn't sleep either. I never can when you're still awake. It's like I can feel it through the wall, that you're up, and then I'm up, and then I have to come see" Claire stopped.

"See what," Brooke said.

"That you're still here." Claire finished the sentence Brooke had started for her, in Brooke's own cadence, a beat early, the way she had begun finishing things this week, and it should have been sisterly and it crawled up the back of Brooke's neck instead.

Brooke made herself talk to her. She asked about nothing, school, a show, whether the squash had been terrible, and she watched her sister answer in the dark and hated what she was doing, because what she was doing was using Claire's need for her, the exact need that frightened her, as bait, to make Claire sit in a dark room and turn her head so a camera could catch her eyes. She was doing to Claire a smaller version of the thing she was afraid something was doing to Claire, and she knew it, and she kept the conversation going anyway.

It took nine minutes. Claire turned toward a sound in the hall, some settling of the house, and went still and focused the way she went still and focused, her head coming up into the thin light off the monitor, and Brooke saw it happen live this time, a green catch at the back of her sister's eyes, there and gone, so fast that if she had not spent an hour looking for it she would have called it nothing.

"What," Claire said, turning back.

"Nothing. Thought I heard Cooper."

She got Claire back to her own room eventually, and closed her door, and pulled the clip off the phone, and there it was, on demand, dated tonight, the flash exactly where she had watched it happen: a girl in a dark room turning her head, and her eyes throwing back a light that was not in the room to throw. Repeatable. Real. Hers now, in triplicate before she slept.

She sat with it for a while and did not feel like she had won anything. She had proven something true and made herself a little more like the thing that had made it true, and she wrote neither of those facts down, because there was no folder for them.

Then she did the thing that frightened her more, which was to listen.

She had a clip she had almost deleted, forty seconds of her own empty bedroom recorded by accident the previous afternoon when she had left her phone propped against the monitor and gone downstairs. She had glanced at it, seen an empty room, and nearly dragged it to the trash. Now she opened it and put in both earbuds and turned it up.

For thirty seconds it was an empty room. Traffic outside. The house settling. Then Claire came into the frame.

She did not know she was being recorded. That was plain from the way she moved, unguarded, none of the careful nearness she performed when Brooke was watching. She crossed to the desk and stood in front of the dark laptop and looked at it, and then she looked at the bed, and then she went and sat on the edge of the bed in the empty room, in the spot she always took, and she put her hands flat on her knees and she was still.

And then, quietly, to no one, in a voice with none of the warmth she used in front of people, Claire said one word.

"Wait."

Brooke pulled the earbuds out.

She sat with the silence of the actual room around her, her own room, the bed six feet away with the ordinary daylight on it. She put one earbud back in and scrubbed to it and listened again, because the discipline of the second look was the only thing keeping her upright. It was there. It was not the house. It was not a word snagged off the street. It was Claire, alone, believing she was alone, saying *wait* to something that was not in the room, in the flat even voice she never used when anyone could hear her.

Downstairs, faintly, Brooke heard her sister laugh at something Sarah said. The warm laugh. The one for people.

She thought about taking the frames and the audio to her mother. She could see exactly how it would go. Sarah would look at a green flash and say it was the flash, and Brooke would say there was no flash, and Sarah would say cameras did strange things in the dark, which was true, cameras did do strange things in the dark, and there was no frame Brooke could show and no clip she could play that would land as anything but a frightened girl building a case against a child who had almost died. Evidence only worked on people who had not already decided. Her mother had decided. Her mother needed to have decided, the same way Brooke had needed the guilt-story an hour ago, and Brooke understood the need well enough that she could not even be angry at it.

So she did not take it downstairs.

She backed up the folder to the cloud and to a drive and to a second drive, three copies, the way she had learned to after losing footage once and swearing never again. She was not doing it for anyone. There was no one to show. She was doing it because a day was coming, she could feel it the way you feel weather, when everyone would say it had not been like this, that Claire had only been a little clingy after a bad flu, that Brooke had been dramatic and unkind, and on that day Brooke wanted the frames to exist outside her own memory, where they could not be argued into nothing.

She opened *Things I Have Seen* and did not add a sentence this time. She added a line and a file path, cold and clerical, the way you logged a shot.

*Her eyes catch light that isn't there. Only in the dark. Only on camera. See folder.*

*She talks to something when she thinks she's alone. She says wait.*

Then she closed the laptop, because Claire had stopped laughing downstairs, and the stairs had gone quiet in the particular way they went quiet when someone was climbing them slowly, and Brooke wanted to be looking at an ordinary room when the door opened.
