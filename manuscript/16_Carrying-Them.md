# Chapter 16

## Carrying Them

Sarah's fever reached one hundred and four before midnight.

Brooke called the hospital twice. The first time she waited forty-three minutes and lost the connection. The second time a nurse asked whether Sarah could breathe, whether she had passed out, whether there was blood in the vomit, and whether another adult was present.

"My sister is here."

"How old is she?"

"Thirteen."

"An adult."

"My dad's out of town."

The nurse went quiet long enough for Brooke to hear voices behind her and an announcement calling for environmental services.

"Emergency departments are operating beyond capacity," she said. "If your mother can keep down small amounts of fluid and remains responsive, manage symptoms at home. Keep family or familiar support persons nearby. Avoid unnecessary separation during recovery."

Brooke looked at Claire, who sat on the bathroom floor with her back against the wall and both hands wrapped around Brooke's ankle.

"What does separation have to do with vomiting?"

"It's part of the updated guidance."

"From who?"

"The department."

"Which department?"

The line clicked. A recorded voice told Brooke to seek immediate care for difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, or signs of severe dehydration.

An alert arrived on Sarah's patient portal a few minutes later.

> RECOVERY SUPPORT NOTICE  
> Patients benefit from calm, familiar surroundings. When possible, avoid unnecessary isolation from household members during illness and recovery.

Brooke took a screenshot.

Sarah vomited again.

The next twelve hours reduced the house to water, temperatures, and the distance between rooms. Brooke kept the thermometer and electrolyte packets on the bathroom counter and wrote down enough to know when the numbers changed.

Sarah's body seemed determined to empty itself beyond what a body could contain. When vomiting paused, diarrhea began. When that stopped, tremors shook the mattress hard enough to tap the headboard against the wall. Brooke changed towels, tied off trash bags, wiped the floor, and learned not to react when Sarah could no longer reach the bathroom in time.

"Don't look at me," Sarah said once.

Brooke was kneeling beside the bed with clean pajama pants. "I have to."

"No, you don't."

"You did this for both of us."

"You were babies."

"Claire threw up in your purse when she was nine."

From the doorway Claire said, "That was carsickness."

Sarah laughed once. The laugh became a dry heave. Brooke held the bowl until it passed.

Afterward Sarah whispered, "You shouldn't have to."

The sentence hurt more than the work. Brooke fastened the clean clothes and pulled the blanket over her. "There's nobody else."

Claire moved closer at that, offended by being excluded even from a burden she would not carry alone.

Claire followed Brooke through every task.

When Brooke went to the kitchen, Claire carried the empty cup. When Brooke returned upstairs, Claire walked so close behind that her socks brushed Brooke's heels. If Brooke closed the bathroom door to help Sarah change, Claire stood outside and turned the handle until Brooke opened it again.

"I need space to move," Brooke said.

"I can help."

"Then get the blue blanket from Mom's room."

"Come with me."

"I can't leave her."

"Then I won't either."

Claire sat beside the tub and watched Sarah shake beneath two towels. She did not hold Sarah's hand. She watched Brooke hold it.

At three in the morning, Sarah opened her eyes and whispered Mark's name.

"He's still at the job," Brooke said.

"Call him."

"I did."

She had called six times. Each went directly to voicemail. The messages she left grew shorter because she could hear herself becoming a child in them.

*Dad, Mom's sick. Call me.*

*It's the same thing Claire had.*

*I don't know if I should drive her.*

*Please call.*

His reply arrived at 3:17.

*Signal came back for a minute. Still at the hotel. Road crew says morning. Keep everyone together until I get there. Love you.*

The message could have been delayed. The road crew could have said morning. He could still be at the hotel. Every sentence gave Brooke something and answered nothing.

She typed, *Call now.*

The message did not deliver.

At four twelve, a voicemail appeared without a missed call.

Mark's voice sounded distant and compressed.

> "Brookie, I got your messages. Listen, keep your mom drinking. Tiny amounts, don't let her gulp it. If she passes out and you can't wake her, call an ambulance. The site manager says we may get the trucks out after daylight. I know I keep saying that. I'm sorry. Tell Sarah I'm trying. Tell Claire I love her. I love you. I'll call when I can."

Behind him, somebody said something too low to understand. Mark paused as if listening. Then the recording ended.

Brooke played the voicemail for Sarah when she woke. Sarah smiled at *Brookie*. Claire asked to hear the part where he said he loved her again.

Brooke replayed it twice. On the third time she listened only to the silence before the message ended.

Sarah curled toward the toilet. Brooke put the phone down and caught the bowl before Sarah knocked it over.

"You're good at this," Sarah whispered when the spasm passed.

"I'm not."

"You are."

Her voice had no strength for an argument. Brooke wiped her face with the corner of a towel.

"I posted another video," Brooke said.

Sarah's eyes remained closed. "I know."

"You heard it."

"Enough."

"I wasn't saying you're not my mother."

Sarah swallowed. Even that hurt. "Not now."

"I need you to know."

"Brooke."

"I don't want another mother." The sentence came out smaller than she intended. "I want you to stop deciding what I can survive. I want you to stop being scared every time I leave. But I don't want somebody else."

Sarah's face tightened. Brooke could not tell whether she was crying or whether fever had made her eyes water.

"I need you," Brooke said.

Sarah moved her hand beneath the towel. Her fingers found Brooke's wrist and rested there without closing.

"I know," she whispered.

Brooke lowered her forehead to the edge of the mattress.

By morning, the symptoms shifted. The vomiting slowed. Sarah became confused about where Mark was and asked twice why Claire was not at school. Sunlight through the blinds made her turn her face toward the wall.

Claire sat on the bedroom floor with a book open in her lap. She had not turned a page.

Brooke checked Sarah's pulse and wrote the number down. When she looked up, Claire was watching her, not Sarah.

"Why don't you sit with Mom?"

"I am."

"You know what I mean."

Claire moved closer to Brooke until their shoulders touched.

Around eleven, Sarah tried to walk to the bathroom by herself.

Brooke heard the bedframe move and came in from the hall in time to catch her against the dresser. Sarah's knees folded. Brooke took her weight beneath the arms and lowered her to the floor rather than trying to hold her upright.

"I can walk," Sarah said.

"You just proved you can't."

"Don't be smart."

"Then stop doing dangerous things."

It was Sarah's sentence, returned in Brooke's voice. Even sick, Sarah recognized it. A weak smile moved across her face and vanished when another tremor went through her.

Claire stood in the doorway.

"Help me get her up," Brooke said.

Claire came forward reluctantly. She placed one hand beneath Sarah's elbow but kept the other on Brooke's back. Together they moved Sarah the few steps to the bathroom. Sarah was not heavy, but her body had stopped helping. Brooke felt every pound of her and thought of all the times Sarah had carried sleeping children from the car without waking them.

"I used to carry both of you," Sarah murmured.

"Not at the same time."

"You don't remember."

"I was ten when Claire was born."

"Then maybe I remember wrong."

The admission frightened Brooke more than confusion would have. Sarah had always defended her memory.

They settled her beside the toilet. Claire crouched against Brooke instead of their mother. Sarah looked at the way Claire clung to Brooke's sleeve.

"Give your sister room," she said.

Claire did not move.

"Claire."

The second time, something in Sarah's voice reached her. Claire released the fabric and sat back. Brooke watched both of them. Sarah's eyes were unfocused with fever, but the instruction had been automatic and maternal. For a moment the house contained its old arrangement: Sarah setting a limit, Claire resenting it, Brooke relieved not to be the only person who saw the need.

Then Sarah leaned over the toilet and the moment ended.

The purge changed Sarah's appetite before it restored it. Near noon she asked for canned beets.

Brooke thought she had misheard. "We don't have beets."

"Back of the pantry."

Sarah bought them once a year for Mark and complained that opening the can made the kitchen smell like wet dirt. Brooke found two cans behind the soup.

"You can't eat that."

"I want them."

"You haven't kept down water."

Sarah closed her eyes. "Then later."

Claire stood beside Brooke in the pantry doorway. "I like them now too."

They looked at each other. Claire smiled as if this were a shared family preference they had only just discovered.

Brooke moved the cans to the highest shelf.

Brooke changed the sweat-soaked sheets while Claire remained in the bathroom. By noon, Sarah's fever began falling. The relief was so physical that Brooke had to sit on the bathroom floor before her legs did it for her.

She had been awake for more than twenty-seven hours.

"Sleep," Claire said.

"I need to check her again at one."

"I'll wake you."

Brooke looked at her.

"I can tell time," Claire said, with enough irritation to sound thirteen.

Brooke set three alarms anyway. She lay on top of Sarah's bed with her shoes on, leaving space between herself and her mother. Claire sat on the floor near the door. Cooper remained in the hall, unwilling to enter.

Brooke dreamed of Saint Vincent's. In the dream, every patient held the same red cup and every person turned toward her at once.

When she woke, the room was dark.

Her phone lay beneath her hand. The alarms had been dismissed.

Claire sat on the edge of the mattress.

She was not looking at Sarah. She was looking at Brooke.

"What time is it?"

"Two sixteen."

Brooke pushed herself upright. "Why didn't you wake me?"

"You needed to sleep."

"I had to check Mom."

"I checked her."

Sarah lay still on her side. Brooke reached across the blanket and pressed two fingers to her neck. Her skin was cooler. Her pulse was steady.

"What did you check?"

"If she was breathing."

"How?"

"I watched."

Brooke's mouth was dry. "From where?"

Claire glanced at the indentation beside Brooke. "Here."

"You sat here the whole time?"

"You kept moving in your sleep."

Sarah stirred. Claire turned toward her at last. Sarah opened her eyes without confusion and looked first at Brooke, then Claire, then the dark window.

"What time is it?" she asked.

"After two."

"In the afternoon?"

"Yes."

Sarah pushed herself up. Brooke reached to help, but Sarah was already sitting. Her hair stuck to her face and her lips were cracked, yet the feverish slackness was gone. She looked exhausted and entirely present.

"You need water," Brooke said.

"I need a shower."

"You almost passed out this morning."

"Then I smell worse than I thought."

It was exactly the kind of thing Sarah would say. Brooke laughed, and the laugh broke halfway through.

Sarah saw it. Her face softened. "Come here."

Brooke did.

She leaned into Sarah carefully, mindful of the sick heat still trapped in the sheets. Sarah wrapped both arms around her. For one moment Brooke let herself be held without measuring the pressure.

"You took care of me," Sarah said into her hair.

"There wasn't anybody else."

"There was you."

Behind Brooke, Claire shifted closer on the bed. Sarah opened one arm to include her, and Claire pressed herself into the space immediately.

Brooke was between them.

Sarah kissed the top of her head. "We're all right."

Cooper stood in the doorway, watching.

Sarah saw him and patted the mattress.

Cooper did not enter.

Sarah's hand remained raised for a moment. Hurt crossed her face before she lowered it.

"He'll come around," Claire said.

Sarah looked at Claire. Something quiet passed between them, not a signal Brooke could name, only the ease of two people arriving at the same conclusion.

"Of course he will," Sarah said.

Brooke was still inside her arms. She noticed that Sarah had begun rubbing slow circles between her shoulder blades, the exact motion she used when Brooke was little and homesick after sleepovers. It soothed her before she could decide whether she wanted it to.

That was the first thing Sarah's recovery gave back: the knowledge of how to make Brooke stay.
