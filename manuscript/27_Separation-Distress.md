# Chapter 27

## Separation Distress

Brooke made it upstairs by pretending she was too tired to fight.

Sarah followed close enough to catch her if she fell. Claire carried the backpack after Brooke finally let her take it. Cooper climbed the stairs slowly, pausing before each step that required his sore paw.

"Bathroom first," Sarah said. "Then sleep."

"I want my bag."

Claire held it out.

Sarah took it before Brooke could.

"I'll bring it in after you change."

"There are camera batteries in there."

"They'll still be there."

Brooke looked at the hand gripping the strap. "Give it to me."

Sarah did.

The immediate surrender made Brooke hesitate. Sarah had learned that obvious resistance gave Brooke something to push against.

"Thank you," Brooke said.

She entered her room with Cooper and closed the door.

Sarah's hand struck the other side before the latch caught.

"Keep it open."

"I'm changing."

"Use the bathroom."

"The bathroom doesn't lock."

No answer came for a second.

"I will keep Claire downstairs," Sarah said.

"I need my room."

"Brooke."

"Five minutes."

Sarah's fingers remained around the edge of the door.

Brooke said, "If you force it, I'll scream."

The fingers disappeared.

Brooke closed and locked the door.

She moved before Sarah could reconsider. Desk chair beneath the handle. Low dresser dragged across the carpet and wedged behind it. The sound was loud enough that pretending became impossible.

"Brooke," Sarah said.

Brooke opened the backpack. Camera. External drive. Charger. Cash. The photographs of the letter. Everything still there.

Her phone died as she plugged it in.

"Move the furniture."

Brooke checked the window. It opened. The loosened screen lifted without resistance.

Cooper watched her from the rug.

"Not yet," she whispered.

Sarah knocked once. "You came home because you knew you needed us."

Brooke powered on the camera instead of answering. The battery showed forty-one percent. She set it on the desk facing the door and pressed record.

"It is Sunday morning," she said quietly. "I got home after the police and other people tried to return me. My mother let me in. Now she won't let me leave my room unless I leave the door open."

"That is not what I said," Sarah called.

Brooke looked at the camera. "She can hear me."

Claire sat down outside the door. Brooke recognized the soft impact against the wall.

"I didn't take your journal," Claire said.

"I believe you."

"Then open the door."

"Those are different things."

Claire began to cry.

Sarah said, "Your sister has been terrified."

"I was terrified too."

"We know. Let us help."

Brooke connected her phone to the charger. One percent. She waited until the emergency-call screen appeared.

The dispatcher answered on the second ring.

"Nine-one-one. What is the address of your emergency?"

Brooke gave the street number before Sarah could hear enough to interfere.

"I'm locked in my bedroom. My mother has been sick and she won't let me leave."

"Is your mother threatening you?"

"She took my things. She's outside the door. My sister is too."

"Are there weapons in the home?"

Brooke looked toward the floor, as though she could see through it to the garage. "A shotgun. It's locked in a safe."

Sarah stopped talking.

The dispatcher said, "Try to remain calm."

"Send someone."

"A unit has been notified. Do not antagonize your mother while you wait."

"She can hear this."

"That is all right."

Brooke gripped the phone. "No, it isn't."

"Your mother may be experiencing separation distress. Staying inside with her is the safest immediate option."

The words matched the school handout.

"I am already inside."

"Good. Remain where you are."

"She is trying to get through the door."

Sarah had gone completely silent.

"Do not make any sudden attempt to leave. She is still your mother, Brooke."

Brooke stopped breathing.

She had given the address. She had not given her name.

"How do you know who I am?"

The dispatcher did not answer the question.

"Brooke, officers are managing multiple family-reunification calls. If your mother is calm, do not escalate the situation."

"You didn't send anyone."

"A unit has been notified."

"Is one coming?"

A pause.

"Remain with your family."

The call ended.

Brooke stared at the screen. Two percent battery.

Outside the door, Claire whispered, "They said to stay."

"You heard one side of it."

"I heard enough."

Brooke returned to the camera.

"If you're seeing this," she began, then stopped.

The sentence sounded like an ending. She was not ready to give the day one.

She started again.

"The police know who I am. The dispatcher knew before I told her. They are not coming. If somebody in your house got sick and recovered, don't let the word recovered make the decision for you. Don't wait for them to look wrong."

Her voice shook. She left it in.

"They remember you. They love you. That doesn't mean they will let you leave."

Brooke saved the recording without uploading it. There was no time to check metadata, remove the address, or decide whether posting would lead more people to the house.

Sarah scratched one fingernail down the door.

"I am trying very hard not to frighten you," she said.

"Then go downstairs."

"You came back because outside was dangerous."

"You are dangerous."

The scratching stopped.

Claire said, "Don't say that."

"It's true."

Sarah's voice changed. Not louder. Less careful.

"After everything I have done to keep you safe."

"You canceled the workshop. You took my phone. You called the school. You took my key. You reported me missing."

"Because you needed me."

"Because you needed me here."

The door moved inward half an inch. The chair legs scraped.

Brooke shoved the dresser harder against it.

Claire began pleading. "Just let me hug you. One time. I won't hold too hard."

"No."

"I promise."

"You can't promise that."

Claire's hand appeared beneath the door, fingers searching across the carpet. Cooper approached, sniffed, then backed away.

Claire withdrew her hand before he could growl.

The pressure against the door increased. Wood complained around the latch.

Brooke picked up the camera, the external drive, and the phone. She put them in the backpack and pulled the straps tight.

"Mom, if you come through that door, I'm going out the window."

The pressure stopped.

Sarah said, "You will fall."

"Then don't come through."

"You could break your neck."

"Then stay out."

For a moment, nobody moved.

Sarah began to cry.

The sound was the same as every exhausted, frightened cry Brooke had heard through childhood walls. Brooke pictured Sarah sitting on the hall carpet, one hand over her mouth, trying not to make the girls worry.

"I don't know how to help you anymore," Sarah said.

Brooke pressed both hands to her eyes.

"Let me leave."

"I can't."

The honesty hurt worse than another plea.

"Why?"

"Because I love you."

Brooke lowered her hands.

The hall went quiet.

Not the quiet of waiting. The quiet of absence.

"Mom?"

No answer.

Claire was gone too.

Brooke waited beside the window. One minute. Two. The house settled around her. Pipes clicked. A vehicle passed on the street.

Maybe Sarah had listened. Maybe the last honest answer had broken something open.

Then metal struck metal in the garage.

Brooke knew the sound because she had mistaken the file cabinet for it two days earlier.

The safe door.

She lifted the window.

"Cooper."

He came to her despite the bad paw.

Brooke removed the screen and pushed the backpack onto the porch roof. Cold air filled the room. She climbed onto the sill and lowered one foot to the shingles.

Footsteps reached the stairs.

Sarah was coming back.

Brooke climbed out and turned to Cooper. "Come."

He put his front paws on the sill, then recoiled from the drop.

The bedroom doorknob turned.

"Brooke," Sarah called. "Move away from the window."

Brooke grabbed Cooper's harness and guided his front legs over the sill. He scrambled, nails striking wood, and Brooke pulled until his weight carried him onto the roof beside her.

The door shuddered.

"I don't want to hurt you," Sarah said.

Brooke crouched below the window line, one hand locked in Cooper's harness.

The shotgun fired.

The blast punched through the latch side of the bedroom door. Wood and metal struck the far wall. The chair overturned. The dresser shifted.

Cooper tried to bolt down the roof. Brooke held him until the echo left the air.

Inside, Claire screamed.

Sarah said, "Brooke?"

The fear in her voice was real.

Brooke moved.

She slid down the wet shingles with Cooper beside her, caught the gutter, and lowered herself toward the porch railing. Her boots struck wood. Cooper jumped past her into the flower bed and yelped when his sore paw landed.

Brooke dropped after him.

The upstairs window opened wider behind them.

"Brooke, wait."

She ran through the side gate and into the street.

No siren came. No cruiser turned the corner. The house remained lit behind her, warm in every window.

Brooke ran until the second gunshot she expected never came.

Then headlights switched on ahead.
