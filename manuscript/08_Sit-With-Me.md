# Chapter 8: Sit With Me

Claire began following Brooke on Tuesday morning.

At first it was easy to explain. She had been sick. The hospital had separated them. Brooke had promised not to go anywhere and then spent hours behind a set of double doors she could not control.

Claire waited outside the bathroom while Brooke brushed her teeth. She sat on Brooke's bed while Brooke dressed. Downstairs, she moved from chair to counter whenever Brooke crossed the kitchen.

"You're hovering," Brooke said.

"I'm eating."

Claire held a forkful of eggs. Sarah had made them again because Claire asked.

"You can eat from a chair."

"I like it here."

Here meant close enough that their sleeves touched.

Sarah watched them over her coffee. "She had a frightening weekend."

"So did I. I'm not standing on anybody."

Claire smiled. "You can if you want."

Brooke moved to the other side of the island. Claire followed with her plate.

Cooper rose from beneath the table and left the kitchen.

Sarah kept both girls home from school. Claire's pediatrician wanted twenty-four symptom-free hours, and Sarah said Brooke needed sleep. Instead Brooke spent the morning editing while Claire occupied the spare chair beside her desk.

Brooke tried to restore the old distance by giving Claire work. She copied the hospital clips into a new folder and showed her how to add plain labels without opening any of the files.

"Date, time, place," Brooke said. "No opinions."

"You mean no fun."

"You used to beg to help."

"I used to be ten."

For fifteen minutes Claire complained in recognizable ways. She misspelled Saint Vincent's, blamed the keyboard, and renamed one clip *RED BARF HALLWAY* before Brooke made her change it. Brooke relaxed enough to concentrate. This was what Claire had meant about logging. They had spent whole rainy afternoons this way, Brooke shaping a story while Claire did a minor job and acted as if she had been exploited by a major studio.

Then Brooke noticed that Claire had stopped working. Her hands rested in her lap. She was watching Brooke's face reflected in the dark edge of the monitor.

"What?"

"Nothing."

"You haven't labeled anything in five minutes."

"I like watching you."

Brooke turned back to the screen. "That is a deeply creepy sentence."

Claire laughed because Brooke had made it a joke. A moment later she rolled her chair closer, until the wheels touched Brooke's.

"The clicking is annoying," Claire said.

"Then leave."

"No."

"Compelling strategy."

"Sit with me downstairs."

"I'm working."

"You can work downstairs."

Brooke turned from the monitor. "Why?"

Claire's answer came too quickly. "Because I asked."

She smiled after saying it, trying to make the sentence lighter. Brooke recognized the adjustment. Claire had heard herself sound demanding.

"Give me twenty minutes."

"Ten."

"Thirty now."

"That's not how bargaining works."

Claire watched the screen while Brooke reviewed footage from before the hospital: Claire at lunch leaving the sandwich untouched, Cooper moving behind Brooke's chair, Claire on the stairs reaching for her hand. In the corner of the monitor, the current Claire leaned closer.

"Don't use the part where I throw up."

"I didn't."

"Promise?"

"It's your face. You get a veto."

Claire relaxed. "Okay."

The ordinary response made Brooke feel ashamed of the question forming in her mind.

At noon, Sarah took a work call upstairs. Brooke carried her laptop to the sofa. Claire curled beside her beneath the heavy blanket, shoulder pressed along Brooke's arm. Each time Brooke shifted, Claire shifted too.

The highest-rated comment beneath *My sister got sick* called Claire's behavior a trauma response and told Brooke to be kind. Brooke wanted it to be right.

Other comments argued about the red medicine. One person claimed it was an antiviral trial. Another said it was a vitamin compound used after severe dehydration. Three accounts posted the same paragraph about supporting recovered family members, including the same missing comma. Brooke clicked through to see whether they were bots. One belonged to a middle-aged man who posted fishing photographs. One belonged to a local church. The third had been created that morning.

She saved screenshots, then felt ridiculous. Repeated language was not evidence of anything except copying, and copying was what the internet did best.

Claire fell asleep against her. Brooke waited until her breathing deepened, then eased one arm free and stood.

Brooke made it to the kitchen before Claire appeared in the doorway with the blanket around her shoulders.

"You left."

"I got water."

"You could've told me."

Cooper stood in the hall. Claire crouched and held out both hands. "Are you done being weird?"

He turned his head away.

"Cooper."

Brooke touched his collar. The fur beneath it felt warm. "Come on, buddy."

Claire reached for the side of his face.

The growl came without warning.

It was lower than a bark and louder than the sound he had made outside Claire's room. His lips did not lift. His body simply became rigid beneath Brooke's hand.

Claire froze.

Sarah appeared at the top of the stairs with her headset still on. "What happened?"

"He growled at me," Claire said.

Cooper backed behind Brooke.

"Did somebody step on him?" Sarah asked. "Startle him?"

"Claire touched him."

Sarah came down and checked his paws, collar, and ears. Cooper tolerated everything while watching Claire.

"Maybe he's sick," Sarah said.

"He isn't."

"You don't know that."

Claire remained crouched in the hall. Her face had gone blank with hurt.

"It's okay," Brooke told her.

Claire looked at Cooper. "I didn't do anything."

She did not shout at him or reach again. She stood slowly and moved beside Brooke instead, close enough that their hands brushed.

Sarah said they would call the veterinarian. Brooke nodded. Maybe there was a sore place beneath his fur, or some smell from the hospital that had not washed away.

The veterinarian could not see Cooper until Friday. A technician asked whether his eating, drinking, and bathroom habits had changed. Sarah answered no to everything.

"Any new people or animals in the home?"

Sarah looked at Claire and then away. "My daughter came home from the hospital."

"Different smells can make dogs cautious. Medication, disinfectant, stress hormones. Give him space and don't force an interaction."

Brooke wrote the last sentence in her notes.

Claire saw. "Seriously?"

"It's what she said."

"About the dog."

"Yes."

"You're making another list about me."

Brooke locked her phone. "I'm writing down the explanation too."

"So you can prove Mom wrong later?"

Sarah ended the call and set the phone on the counter. "Enough."

Claire turned away first. She opened the pantry and stood looking inside, not choosing anything. Brooke remembered her at eight years old, furious because Brooke had made a chart tracking how often Claire borrowed her clothes. The chart had lasted two days before Sarah made Brooke take it down. Claire had called it creepy then too.

Brooke deleted the note about the veterinarian. Five minutes later, in the bathroom, she restored it from the trash.

At dinner Claire functioned so normally Brooke began to distrust her own discomfort. She complained about homework she had not started. She argued that a twenty-four-hour fever deserved at least three days without chores. She took Brooke's last dinner roll and denied it while chewing.

Then Sarah stood to refill her water, and Claire stood too.

"Where are you going?" Sarah asked.

Claire looked surprised. "Nowhere."

"Then sit."

Claire sat. Her gaze followed Sarah to the sink and back. When Sarah returned, Claire moved her chair close enough that its leg touched Brooke's.

Brooke waited for Sarah to notice. Sarah noticed everything. A damp towel on the floor could summon her from another level of the house.

This time Sarah kept eating.

That night, Claire appeared in Brooke's doorway carrying her pillow.

"No."

"I didn't ask yet."

"You brought a pillow."

"Can I sleep on the floor?"

"You have a bed."

"I don't want to be alone."

Brooke looked past her toward the dark hall. Cooper lay at the foot of Brooke's bed, head raised.

"I'll sit with you until you fall asleep," Brooke said.

Claire considered this. "In my room?"

"That is where your bed lives."

Brooke followed her across the hall. Claire climbed beneath the covers and took Brooke's wrist, holding it lightly against the mattress.

"You don't have to hold on."

"Do what?"

"Hold me here."

Claire loosened her fingers. "I'm just holding your hand."

Brooke stayed until Claire's eyes closed, then slipped her hand free.

In the hall, she waited.

Claire's breathing remained slow. Brooke crossed to her room, eased the door nearly shut, and sat on the edge of her bed without turning on a light. Cooper lifted his head from the rug. For the first time all day, nobody was touching her or asking where she was going.

The relief lasted less than a minute.

Claire's door opened. Bare feet crossed the hall and stopped outside Brooke's room.

Brooke watched the narrow dark line between the door and frame. Claire did not knock. She did not call her name. She stood there long enough that Cooper rose and placed himself between the bed and the entrance.

At last Claire whispered, "Brooke?"

Brooke could have pretended to be asleep. Instead she said, "I'm here."

The door opened immediately.

Claire did not enter. She looked at Cooper, then at Brooke, and held the pillow against her chest. "You said you'd stay until I fell asleep."

"You fell asleep."

"I woke up."

"People do that."

"Come back."

Brooke went because it was late, because Claire had almost died, and because every reason Sarah offered still sounded reasonable when Claire stood in the doorway looking thirteen. This time Brooke sat in the desk chair rather than on the bed. Claire watched her until her eyes closed.

Brooke counted to three hundred before leaving.

At one hundred and twelve, Claire smiled without opening her eyes.

"You're still here," she murmured.

Brooke stopped counting.
