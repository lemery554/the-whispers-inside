# Chapter 5: Empty

Sarah moved Claire to the upstairs bathroom because it had tile, a lock that could be left open, and a tub close enough to catch what the toilet did not.

Brooke gathered the ruined towels by their corners and carried them to the laundry room. The smell followed her down the stairs. She loaded everything into the washer, added too much detergent, and stood with her hand on the lid while the machine filled. Above her, Claire vomited again. The sound traveled through the pipes and walls without losing anything.

Brooke opened the state health department page on her phone. A yellow banner covered the top.

**GASTROINTESTINAL ILLNESS: HOME CARE AND WARNING SIGNS**

*Encourage frequent small sips of clear liquid. Do not force food. Contact a medical provider for persistent vomiting, signs of severe dehydration, confusion, difficulty waking, blood in vomit or stool, or fever above 103°F.*

Beneath the list, a new notice warned that emergency departments were experiencing unusually high volume. Patients with mild symptoms should call before arriving.

Nothing about Claire felt mild. Brooke took screenshots anyway, because pages could change and because having the list in her hand felt almost like having instructions.

When she returned upstairs, Sarah knelt beside Claire on the bathroom floor. Claire leaned against the tub in a T-shirt and underwear, her sweatshirt abandoned near the door. Sweat shone along her arms. A plastic basin sat in her lap.

"I want my bed," Claire said.

"As soon as your stomach settles," Sarah told her.

"It settled."

Her body contradicted her before the sentence ended. Sarah caught the basin and held Claire's hair away from her face. Brooke stopped in the doorway, useless with a glass of water in one hand and her phone in the other.

There was almost nothing left to come up. Claire retched until her shoulders shook, bringing up thin strings of yellow liquid that gathered in the bottom of the basin. When it stopped, she sagged against Sarah.

"Rinse your mouth," Sarah said. Claire shook her head.

"Just a little."

"It tastes wrong."

"It's water."

"I don't want it."

Brooke crouched and held out the glass. "One sip. Then you can yell at us again."

Claire looked at her through wet strands of hair. "I'm not yelling."

"You're doing a weak version. It's embarrassing."

The corner of Claire's mouth moved. She accepted the glass, wet her lips, and shoved it back so suddenly water spilled across Brooke's wrist.

"No."

"Okay."

"Get the lemon ice pop taste out."

"You already threw it up."

Claire stared at her. "I can still taste it."

Sarah glanced at Brooke. Neither said what they were thinking: the ice pop had come up hours ago, before the fever climbed, before the towels on the landing.

Brooke checked the warning-sign list. Persistent vomiting. Confusion. Difficulty keeping fluids down.

"We should go in," she said.

Sarah wiped Claire's face with a damp washcloth. "We should call first."

"She's dehydrated."

"She's been vomiting for less than an hour."

"She can't drink."

"And the emergency room is asking people not to come unless they have to."

"The page says confusion."

"She is exhausted."

Claire closed her eyes. "I'm here."

Sarah placed a hand against her cheek. "I know, honey."

Brooke did not say that Claire had forgotten Sarah was beside her earlier. Sarah had seen it.

Sarah called the pediatrician's after-hours line. A recording advised anyone experiencing severe dehydration or altered mental status to call 911 or proceed to the nearest emergency department. For all other concerns, they could leave a message for the on-call provider. Sarah left Claire's name, temperature, symptoms, and callback number with the calm precision of someone placing an order. Then they waited.

Brooke searched beneath the state notice while Sarah replaced the damp cloth on Claire's neck. One comment said Saint Vincent's had stopped letting relatives beyond triage. Another showed three people sitting together beneath the emergency-room sign. Contradiction had become the only detail every report shared.

She called Saint Vincent's main number. A recording thanked callers for their patience, warned of extended emergency waits, and repeated that the hospital remained open. The menu offered no choice for asking whether a thirteen-year-old would be separated from her family. Brooke pressed zero until the system disconnected her.

On the second call, she chose patient information. A woman answered after seven minutes and said she could not discuss emergency intake procedures because they were changing by the hour.

"Can two family members stay with a child?" Brooke asked.

"The clinical team will decide based on space and safety."

"What does that mean?"

"It means bring one adult if possible."

Brooke looked through the bathroom doorway at Sarah supporting Claire's weight. "What happens to the other person?"

The woman paused. "They wait."

Brooke thanked her because the woman sounded as tired as Sarah.

A video posted twenty minutes earlier showed shoes, chair legs, and a white paper cup tipped on its side in a crowded waiting room. A vivid red line ran from the cup toward the filmer's sneaker. Someone out of frame retched.

Brooke watched six seconds, muted it, and locked her screen. Claire was sick in front of her. The footage did not make Claire safer or tell Sarah what to do. For the first time since the clips began, saving more felt like refusing to look at what was already in the room.

At eight, Claire's fever was 102.8. At eight-twenty, she vomited after one teaspoon of electrolyte drink. At eight-forty, diarrhea forced Sarah to close the bathroom door, leaving Brooke in the hall with fresh clothes and the sound of Claire crying that she was sorry.

The illness erased privacy by degrees. First Sarah asked Brooke to bring clean underwear. Then Claire needed help standing. Then the bath mat went into a trash bag and Sarah stopped closing the door all the way because she needed Brooke near enough to hear instructions. Claire protested each loss until exhaustion made protest more expensive than embarrassment.

Brooke learned how to knot a trash bag without touching the wet plastic, how to run the shower just long enough to warm the room without filling it with steam, and how to look at Claire's face instead of the mess around her. None of it felt like helping until Sarah asked for something else.

"You have nothing to be sorry for," Sarah said through the door.

"It's gross."

"Bodies are gross. Your sister was gross for most of 2009."

Brooke sat against the opposite wall. "I was three."

"Exactly. No standards."

Claire gave a small laugh that broke in the middle. Brooke pressed the heels of her hands into her eyes.

Cooper lay at the far end of the hall. He had tried twice to come closer when Claire cried, but each time he reached the bathroom threshold, he stopped and retreated. Now he watched the closed door with his chin on the floor.

Brooke lifted her phone and recorded the time, the thermometer on the carpet, the unopened electrolyte bottle, and Cooper's position in the hall. She did not point the camera toward the bathroom. The clips were no longer material for an upload. They were a record for a doctor who might ask when something happened, and for Brooke if morning arrived and everyone began saying it had not been this bad.

Sarah opened the door enough to take the clothes. Her college shirt was damp at the collar, and strands of hair had worked free from the clip at the back of her head. "Put a trash bag in the bathroom bin. Then bring another blanket. I need ten minutes where you're helping me instead of fighting me."

Brooke went for the blanket. At nine-oh-three, Mark called. Sarah put him on speaker from the bathroom counter. His voice broke around the edges, flattened by poor service and the fan Sarah had turned on.

"How high?" he asked.

"One-oh-two point eight," Sarah said. "Maybe higher now. She can't keep fluids down."

"Did you try ice chips?"

"Yes."

"The blue electrolyte stuff she likes?"

"It came back up."

Claire opened her eyes. "I don't like blue." Mark paused. "Since when?"

"It tastes dirty."

"It's sealed," Sarah said.

"Not the bottle. The blue." The fan filled the silence.

Mark said, "I'm leaving as soon as they clear us. If she gets worse, don't wait for me."

"The pediatrician hasn't called back," Sarah replied.

"Call again."

"I did."

"Then take her in."

Brooke looked at Sarah. Sarah turned away from the phone as if Mark could see her expression through the speaker.

"The hospitals are overwhelmed," she said.

"They're still hospitals."

Claire lurched toward the toilet. The phone slid against the counter while Sarah caught her. Mark's voice repeated Claire's name from somewhere beneath the noise.

The call disconnected before anyone said goodbye.

Sarah picked up the phone and called back immediately. Voicemail. She tried again while Claire rinsed her mouth. Voicemail again.

"He said he was leaving," Brooke said.

"He said as soon as they clear them."

"Same thing."

"No, it isn't."

Sarah set the phone faceup on the counter, volume at maximum. For the next hour, every vibration made all three of them look toward it. Most were building alerts from Sarah's work or news notifications Brooke had forgotten to silence.

At nine-twenty, the pediatrician returned the message. The on-call nurse sounded tired and spoke quickly. She asked whether Claire had urinated in the last six hours, whether her mouth was dry, whether she could stand without dizziness, whether she knew where she was. Sarah answered until the last question. "Claire," she said, "tell me where you are."

Claire rested her forehead against the cool side of the tub. "Home."

"Where at home?"

"The room."

"Which room?"

Claire frowned at the toilet, the sink, the bath mat beneath her knees. "The white one." Their bathroom was pale blue.

The nurse told Sarah to take her to the emergency department.

The house became a list: insurance card, phones, clean clothes, plastic bags, medication history, basin. Sarah moved through it without stopping while Brooke obeyed.

Brooke added Claire's temperature note, the state-health screenshots, and the short video of Cooper outside the room. Sarah saw the video thumbnail when Brooke checked the folder.

"Do not show them her throwing up."

"I didn't film that."

Sarah looked at her long enough to make the answer feel like a test. "Good."

Brooke wanted credit for the boundary and hated herself for wanting it.

"Shoes she can put on in the car. Bring the gray blanket. Put Cooper in the kitchen."

At his name, Cooper rose but would not approach Claire. Sarah had helped her into sweatpants and one of Mark's old T-shirts. Claire stood between them in the hallway, swaying slightly.

"Cooper," she whispered. He looked at her.

She held out one trembling hand. "Come here."

His tail lowered. He backed toward the stairs.

Claire began to cry. Not loudly. Tears moved down her face while she kept her hand extended, as though she could not understand why he had refused something so simple.

Brooke stepped between them. "He's scared because everybody's running around."

"Of me?"

"Of Mom. Obviously."

Sarah was in the bedroom finding socks and did not hear. Claire's hand dropped.

Brooke put the phone in her pocket and wrapped the gray blanket around her sister. Claire leaned into her immediately, forehead against Brooke's shoulder.

"You're hot," Brooke said.

"You're mean."

The words were faint but familiar. Brooke tightened the blanket around her. "Yeah."

The apology rose and stopped behind Brooke's teeth. Sarah called from the bedroom that they needed to move.

Claire's body jerked. Brooke caught the basin just before she vomited again.

This time the liquid was almost clear.

By ten, Claire had nothing left. Her stomach continued trying anyway.

Sarah drove. Brooke sat in the back beside Claire with the basin between their feet. Rain glazed the roads and turned every traffic light into a long colored smear across the windows. Claire lay with her head in Brooke's lap, shivering beneath the blanket despite the heat pouring from the vents.

They passed the high school at ten-oh-seven. The parking lot lights were on though the building was empty. A digital sign near the road still announced Monday's basketball game and reminded families that attendance mattered. Beneath it, somebody had taped a sheet of paper over the community-event board. In the headlights Brooke read only two words: *STAY HOME*.

At the next intersection, a pharmacy drive-through line wrapped around the building. People waited beneath umbrellas at the walk-up window. Sarah slowed, considered the turn, and continued toward the hospital.

"We could get nausea medicine," Brooke said.

"Not without knowing what she can take."

"Everybody online says they're giving something red."

Sarah's eyes met hers in the mirror. "Online is not her doctor."

Claire shifted in Brooke's lap. "No red."

Neither of them asked what she meant.

Brooke kept one hand on Claire's shoulder and used the other to refresh the Saint Vincent's emergency page. The estimated wait time had disappeared. A notice said temporary triage procedures were in effect. Families should bring identification, medication lists, and patience.

At the turn for the hospital, traffic stopped.

Red taillights stretched ahead of them in two unmoving lines. An ambulance edged along the shoulder, lights flashing without a siren. Cars shifted just enough to let it pass, then closed behind it.

Sarah gripped the steering wheel. "We're going to get there."

Brooke could not tell whether she was speaking to Claire or herself.

Claire opened her eyes. "Don't let them take me by myself."

"They won't," Sarah said.

It was the first promise of the night that none of them had the power to keep.
