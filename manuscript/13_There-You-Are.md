# Chapter 13: There You Are

The comments were still coming the next morning. Brooke did not read them on the bus. The number beside the upload rose each time she checked, proof enough that other people had started saying the same impossible thing.

At school the water had risen another inch. She could not stop seeing it now. By third period she wanted out of the building the way you want out of a dream, and she went to the front office and said she had a headache and asked to sign out early.

Before she left, she saw Mason standing outside the nurse's office with both hands around another student's wrist. The other boy was not struggling. He was crying into Mason's shoulder while the nurse spoke to them in the same calm tone Ms. Ferro had used.

"Nobody is sending anybody away," the nurse said. "We're keeping everyone together until a parent comes."

The boy lifted his face. "My mom said I could walk."

"Your mother will feel better knowing you aren't alone."

Mason looked across the hall at Brooke. His gaze rested on her without embarrassment. The boy's wrist remained enclosed in his hands.

Brooke took out her phone, then remembered Mr. Vale's question. *Proof of what?* She photographed the nurse's office sign and the time on the wall clock, not the boys. In her notes she wrote exactly what she had seen. She did not write what she thought Mason would do if the boy tried to leave.

The woman at the desk was sympathetic and did not let her go. District guidance required a parent or listed guardian to sign out any student reporting illness, and Sarah could not leave work. The woman offered the nurse's office instead, where people could keep an eye on Brooke rather than send her home alone. She said *alone* like it was the dangerous part.

She lay in the nurse's office until the final bell and then went home, because home was where Claire was, and there was no version of the day that did not end with Claire.

By evening the house had closed around her again.

Sarah made dinner. Claire ate roasted squash and did not comment on it and watched Brooke across the table, not hostile, not anything, just watching, the way she watched now, keeping Brooke in view the way the senior in the library had kept the freshman in view. Sarah talked about ordinary things in an ordinary voice, and it should have been comforting and was not, because the ordinariness had become a thing the house performed at her, a smoothness laid over the top of something to keep her from looking under it.

After dinner Brooke needed to be behind a door.

That was all it was. She was not hiding. She just needed four minutes in a room where no one was watching her, and the hall bathroom was the closest one, and she went in and pushed the little lever lock on the handle and sat on the edge of the tub in the light of the one weak fixture and put her face in her hands and breathed.

She had left her phone charging in her room. She reached toward an empty pocket, then let it go. She was sitting in a bathroom. She did not need to record it.

The knock came soft.

"Brooke?"

"I'm in here."

"I know. Can I come in?"

"I'm using the bathroom."

"You're not, though." Claire's voice was patient and certain on the other side of the door, and the certainty was the first thing that was wrong, because Claire could not have known that, unless she had been standing close enough to the door to hear that Brooke had done nothing but sit down. "You're just sitting. I can come in while you sit."

"No. Go away for two minutes."

There was a pause. Brooke pictured her on the other side of the door, and the picture her mind produced, without her permission, was of Claire standing in the hall exactly the way the freshman had stood outside the second-floor bathroom, backpack off now but otherwise the same, patient, aimed at the door, waiting because waiting was the whole activity.

"I don't want to be out here," Claire said.

"You're fine out there."

"I don't like it. The hall's too big." Her voice did something on *too big,* a small catch, real distress underneath it. "Just let me in. I'll sit on the floor. I won't even talk. I just want to be where you are."

"Claire."

"Please." That was the next rung, and Brooke heard it as a rung even then, the shift from asking to pleading, so smooth it barely had edges. "Please, I'll be so quiet. I don't want to be by myself. Why do you keep going where I can't be."

"Because sometimes people close a door. That's normal. That's allowed."

"I can't be out here." Not angry. Stated. "You keep going where I can't follow and I can't stand it, Brooke, I can't, please open the door."

Brooke stood up. Her heart had started going. "I'm going to open it in a minute. Go wait in my room. Sit on my bed. I'll come find you."

"I want you now."

"Claire, back up from the door."

The handle turned. It caught on the lock, the little lever holding, and it turned again, harder, the mechanism straining, and Brooke watched the handle jerk against its stop and understood with a slow cold clarity that her sister was not going to stop, that the sounds she had put in a document under the heading of things she had seen were about to become a thing she saw.

"Claire, stop. The door's locked, stop turning it."

"Then unlock it."

"Stop."

She did not stop. There was a short silence, the specific silence of someone changing their grip, and then the handle wrenched sideways with a force that had no anger in it at all, an even mechanical force like the grip on Brooke's wrist two nights before, and the lever snapped its travel and something in the jamb gave with a splintering crack, and the door shuddered inward against Brooke's hand where she had put it flat against the wood without deciding to.

She stepped back. The door swung open.

Claire stood in the doorway in the weak hall light. She was not breathing hard. Her face was not the face of a girl who had just broken a door. It was calm, and it was glad, and when she saw Brooke standing there against the sink with nowhere to go, her whole expression opened into a warmth so genuine and so wrong that Brooke felt it in her stomach.

"There you are," Claire said.

She came in. She did not lunge. She crossed the small space the way she crossed any room now, unhurried, certain of her welcome, and she put her arms around Brooke and pressed her face into Brooke's shoulder and held on, and it was a hug, that was the unbearable part, it was a thirteen-year-old hugging her big sister, and the arms around Brooke tightened past the pressure of a hug and kept tightening with that same even patience, and Claire made a small satisfied sound against her collarbone and said, muffled, "I don't like it when there's a door."

Brooke stood rigid with her arms half-raised and did not hug her back and looked over Claire's head at the ruined doorframe, the pale splintered wood, the lever handle hanging at a broken angle. There was blood on it. A dark smear along the metal where Claire had wrenched it, and more of it, she saw now, on Claire's right hand where it lay against Brooke's arm, a torn place across two knuckles welling slowly, leaving a small red print on Brooke's sleeve.

"Claire, your hand."

"It doesn't matter."

"You're bleeding, you cut your hand on the lock."

"It doesn't matter." Claire did not look at it. Her hand was split open and pressed against Brooke's arm, but the injury was not enough to make her release the hug.

Cooper appeared in the doorway.

He did not bark. He came into the small bathroom low and stiff, his whole body a line of tension, and he pushed himself between the two girls, physically, his shoulder wedging in against Brooke's legs, putting his body where the space between the sisters used to be. He did not growl at Claire. He did not snap. He faced her and leaned his weight into Brooke and stood there trembling, insisting on the inch of separation with his own body, and Claire, feeling the dog force his way in against her, loosened her arms and looked down at him.

For one second Brooke thought something would happen to the dog. She got a hand into Cooper's collar without knowing she had moved.

But Claire only looked at him. Whatever it was that had made her break a lock and ignore her own torn hand, it stopped at the dog. She lifted her injured hand away from Brooke, the blood on it dark in the poor light, and she did not touch Cooper, and she did not push him, and something crossed her face that might have been confusion, a flicker of the girl who had grown up with this dog and fed him bread under the table her whole life. Then she stepped back, giving the space Cooper demanded, and the warmth came back over her features.

"He's still weird," Claire said, in a voice that was almost normal, almost her own, and it was that, the almost, that finally undid Brooke's composure.

Sarah came up the stairs then, alerted by the crack of the jamb or by the quality of the quiet. She took in the broken door and the blood and Brooke pinned against the sink with the dog between her and Claire. Her hand closed around the banister. Then she moved to Claire.

"What happened?"

"She broke the lock," Brooke said. Her voice came out flat and careful and much steadier than she felt. "I closed the door and she broke the lock to get in."

Sarah looked once more at the splintered jamb. "The lock was old." She reached for Claire's hand and turned it over, her mouth tightening at the torn knuckles. "Claire, baby, look what you did to yourself. Why would you do that?"

"I wanted to be with Brooke."

"You can't break a door because you want to be with someone."

"She wouldn't let me in."

Sarah looked at Brooke, and there it was, the thing Brooke had been watching arrive for a week, the small terrible pivot where the fact of a broken lock and a bleeding child became, in a loving mother's face, a story about a big sister who kept shutting her frightened little sister out. "Why did you lock the door on her?"

"Because I wanted to be alone for four minutes."

"She almost died last week, Brooke."

"I know that. I keep saying I know that." Brooke heard her own voice climb and pulled it back down, because climbing lost. "Look at her hand. Mom. Look at it. She tore her hand open on the lock and she hasn't looked at it once. Does that seem like a scared kid to you, or does that seem like something else."

Sarah was pressing a towel to Claire's knuckles. Claire watched her do it with mild interest, the way you watch someone else's small injury being tended. "Kids get hurt and don't notice when they're upset," Sarah said. "Adrenaline. It's nothing."

"It's not nothing."

"Go to your room, Brooke." Not cruel. Exhausted. "Please. I can't do this and take care of her hand at the same time. Just go."

Brooke went. She kept her hand in Cooper's collar and drew the dog with her out of the bathroom and down the hall to her own room, and she shut the door, and she stood in the middle of the room with her heart slamming and understood the true horror of the last ten minutes only now that it was over.

She had no footage. Sarah had already called the lock old, and Brooke could feel the scene becoming arguable.

She wrote it down while it was sharp: the turning handle, the blood on the lever, *There you are*, Cooper forcing an inch of space. A written account was not proof, but it was what she had.

Then she made herself add the parts that complicated it. Claire had stopped when Cooper intervened. Claire had not pushed him. Claire had looked confused. The hug had begun gently.

Brooke wanted a clean account in which every detail pointed in the same direction. The truth would not give her one.

*It isn't trauma. I don't know what it is, but trauma does not explain all of it.*

Across the hall, through the wall, she heard the water run in the bathroom while Sarah cleaned the blood off the broken lock. And under the water, very quietly, so quietly that Brooke pressed her ear to the cold wall to be sure, she heard her sister, alone for a moment on the landing while their mother rinsed the towel, cradling the hand she had finally been left alone with, drawing a breath in through her teeth at the sting of it.

So she had felt it. She had felt it the whole time. It had hurt, and she had known it hurt, and it simply had not been enough to make her stop.

Brooke stayed against the wall with Cooper leaning into her legs. Claire had felt the injury. It simply had not mattered until Brooke was no longer within reach.
