# Chapter 14

## Sand

Sarah removed the broken bathroom handle before breakfast.

She set the pieces on the kitchen counter beside a roll of gauze and the receipt from the hardware store, as if arranging them neatly might reduce what had happened to an ordinary household repair. The bent lever was still marked with a dark line of Claire's blood. Sarah had cleaned the rest of it from the jamb, but the wood around the latch remained split and pale.

"Your dad can replace the whole assembly when he gets home," she said.

Brooke looked at the empty rectangle where the handle had been. "When is that?"

Sarah took longer than she should have to fold the receipt. "He texted overnight. They're still waiting on an inspection."

"Can I see it?"

"The text?"

"No, the inspection."

Sarah gave her the look that used to mean Brooke was being difficult for sport. It had not changed, which made Brooke hate herself for studying it. "Yes, the text."

Mark had written at 1:14 a.m.

*Still here. Site inspection got pushed. Signal terrible. Tell the girls I love them.*

No joke. No new promise about tomorrow. Brooke read it twice before returning the phone.

Claire sat at the table with two bandaged knuckles and a bowl of oatmeal crowded with sliced banana. She ate with her left hand. The injured one rested beside the bowl, palm down, as if it belonged to somebody seated next to her.

Cooper had chosen the strip of floor between Brooke's chair and the back door. When Claire dropped a piece of banana near him, he sniffed it and moved closer to Brooke without eating it.

"He doesn't like bananas," Sarah said.

"He ate a whole peel once," Brooke said.

"That doesn't mean he liked it."

Claire smiled at that. It was the kind of small family exchange that should have dissolved the night before. Sarah had made oatmeal. Claire was eating. The bathroom needed a new lock. Nobody had been hospitalized or arrested or found dead. If Brooke told the story wrong, it became a story about a frightened girl, an old handle, and a dog who had gotten underfoot.

She took a picture of the broken lever before Sarah threw it away.

Claire watched her do it. "Are you going to put that online?"

"No."

"Why take it?"

"Because it happened."

"You were there."

"So were you."

Claire considered this. "You could've opened the door."

Sarah stopped wiping the counter.

Brooke looked at the white gauze around Claire's hand. "You could've waited four minutes."

"I tried."

"No, you asked for maybe thirty seconds and then broke the lock."

"It felt longer."

Sarah put the cloth in the sink. "We're not doing this again before school."

Brooke laughed. "She's going to school?"

"Both of you are."

"She broke a door."

"And I am dealing with it."

Claire finished the oatmeal and scraped the side of the bowl. "I feel fine."

That was the phrase everyone trusted now. The anchors said it. The hospital notices said it. Parents wrote it beneath Brooke's uploads as if feeling fine answered every question about what happened after the fever went away.

Sarah told Claire to take her antibiotic ointment to the nurse. Brooke did not point out that there was no medicine for whatever had made the injury unimportant. She went upstairs, packed her camera, and waited until she heard Claire enter her own room before leaving hers.

At school, Claire stayed close enough that Brooke could feel her without looking.

She appeared outside Brooke's first-period classroom before the bell, leaning against the lockers in Brooke's green flannel. The shirt had vanished from Brooke's chair sometime after midnight.

"Give it back," Brooke said.

"I'm cold."

"You have clothes."

"I like this one."

Brooke reached for the sleeve. Claire caught her wrist with the bandaged hand.

The grip was immediate and firm. Claire did not squeeze hard enough to hurt. She only prevented Brooke from stepping away, her thumb resting above Brooke's pulse while students passed around them.

"Let go."

"Walk me downstairs."

"Your class is downstairs. Mine is here."

"You can come back."

The warning bell rang. A teacher at the far end of the hall called for everyone to move. Claire did not look toward the voice.

"You're going to make me late," Brooke said.

"Then we'll both be late."

Two girls Brooke recognized from Claire's grade came around the corner holding hands. Not the loose, swinging grip friends used. Their fingers were interlaced tightly, their shoulders touching despite the width of the hall. One of them glanced at Brooke and Claire and smiled with quick recognition, as if they all belonged to the same private arrangement.

Brooke pulled once. Claire's hand tightened.

"Claire."

"Just downstairs."

Brooke walked her downstairs.

By lunch, Claire had found her again. She slid into the seat beside Brooke in the library and pushed her chair close enough that their knees touched beneath the table. The librarian glanced up, saw two sisters together, and returned to her computer.

Brooke had begun choosing public rooms because Claire behaved better in them. That morning proved the strategy had an expiration date.

"Don't you have lunch?" Brooke asked.

"I brought it."

Claire took a plastic container from her bag. Inside were cold roasted mushrooms and squash left from dinner, the vegetables Sarah used to hide beneath Claire's pasta because otherwise she would pick them out. Claire ate a mushroom with her fingers.

"You hate those."

"Not anymore."

"Why?"

Claire shrugged. "They're good."

Brooke opened her laptop. A notification count covered the corner of the upload page, but she did not click it. "I'm working."

"I can sit while you work."

"I need to be alone."

Claire's chewing slowed. "Why?"

"Because I do."

"You keep saying that like it explains something."

"It explains enough."

Brooke moved to the chair across the table. Claire moved too. Brooke gathered the laptop and camera bag and stood.

Claire caught the strap.

The camera bag stopped against Brooke's shoulder. Claire remained seated, one hand wrapped around the black webbing, calm as if she had reached out to keep a book from falling.

"Let go."

"Sit down."

"You're doing it again."

"I'm not hurting you."

"That isn't the rule."

Claire looked genuinely puzzled. "What rule?"

The librarian shushed someone near the printers. Beyond the windows, wind moved through the bare branches above the athletic field. Brooke thought about calling the librarian over. She imagined explaining the bathroom lock, the bleeding hand, the grip on the stairs, and the answer she would receive: Claire had been very ill. Claire needed reassurance. Brooke should be patient.

She lowered the bag to the table but did not sit.

"Do you remember the beach?" she asked.

Claire blinked.

"When you were six. You buried my legs."

"I remember."

"What did you say?"

Claire held the strap.

"You kept rebuilding the sand every time the water knocked it down," Brooke said. "You were furious at the ocean."

"It was ruining it."

The answer came quickly, in Claire's old offended rhythm. Brooke felt a painful spark of hope.

"You told me I lived there now."

Claire's gaze moved from the bag to Brooke's face. "You did."

"I didn't. The tide came in."

"You stayed until I was done."

"Because you cried when I tried to move."

"I didn't cry."

"You cried so hard Dad offered to bury himself instead."

A breath of a laugh left Claire. It was small and unwilling and hers.

Brooke leaned closer. "You put shells all around me. Mom said we had to leave them because something might still live inside. You made me help carry every one back to the water."

Claire's fingers loosened on the strap.

"We were good that day," Brooke said. She did not know what she meant until the words were out. Good sisters, maybe. Good daughters. Good versions of themselves, preserved in a frame before either one knew to distrust memory.

Claire's eyes filled.

The change was so sudden Brooke stopped speaking. Tears gathered along Claire's lower lashes and slid down both cheeks. Claire lifted her injured hand and touched one as if checking whether it was water.

"Why am I crying?"

Brooke sat across from her. "Because you remember."

"I remember the beach."

"And before you got sick. You remember what I said to you."

Claire's face tightened. Her hand returned to the camera strap, but she did not pull.

Brooke heard the sentence again in her own voice, precise as recorded sound. *At least you never have to wonder why your real mother didn't want you.*

"I was angry," Brooke said. "I wanted to hurt you, so I said the worst thing I could think of. I'm sorry."

Claire stared at her through tears. "Mom is your real mother."

The exact answer from the hall. Brooke's throat closed.

"I know."

"Then why would you say that?"

For a second Brooke was back on the stairs with the warm flannel in her hands. "Because I wonder anyway."

Claire began to cry harder, silently now. Her shoulders barely moved. She looked frightened by the tears, not by Brooke. The hand on the strap opened.

Brooke could have taken the bag and run.

Instead she reached across the table. "Claire?"

Claire looked at Brooke's hand but did not take it. Something in her face moved beneath the calm, a struggle without language. Her lips parted. Brooke waited for her name, a warning, anything she could use as proof later.

Claire said, "I don't want you to leave."

The sentence was soft and ordinary. It was also the same thing she always said now.

Brooke drew her hand back.

Claire wiped her cheeks. Confusion remained for a few seconds after the tears stopped. Then the confusion smoothed away. She closed her fingers around the strap again, gently this time.

"You can work," she said. "I'll be quiet."

Brooke sat because the bell would ring soon, because the librarian was watching now, because Claire had let go once and Brooke did not know whether leaving would erase whatever had surfaced. They remained across from one another until lunch ended. Claire ate the rest of the mushrooms. Neither mentioned the beach again.

At dismissal, Brooke tried to leave through the west doors without Claire.

It was not a plan so much as a test. Claire's last class was on the opposite side of the building. Brooke left before the final announcements ended, cut through the art hallway, and reached the student lot with Cooper's leash looped inside her camera bag for after school. She had crossed half the lot when Claire called her name.

Brooke kept walking.

Claire ran after her. Not fast at first. When Brooke reached the sidewalk without turning, Claire's shoes struck harder behind her.

"Brooke, wait."

Brooke stopped beside the crossing guard. A police cruiser idled at the curb with its lights off. The officer stood near a minivan talking to a man and a crying boy. The boy was trying to get into the back seat while the man held both his shoulders and insisted they needed to go home together.

Claire caught Brooke's backpack and pulled.

"I was looking for you."

"I know."

"You left."

"School ended. People leave."

Claire's face tightened. Tears threatened again, but they did not have the bewildered quality of the library. These tears had a purpose Brooke understood. They made adults look.

The crossing guard did. "Everything okay?"

"She keeps running away from me," Claire said.

"I'm walking home."

The police officer glanced over. He was young, with tired red skin above his collar. "You two sisters?"

"Yes," Claire said.

Brooke said, "She won't let go of my bag."

The officer approached without urgency. Behind him, the crying boy had been guided into the minivan beside the man. The officer looked at Claire's bandaged hand and then at Brooke.

"Lot of people on edge right now," he said. "Best thing you can do is stay together and get home."

"She broke a bathroom lock yesterday because I closed the door."

The crossing guard's expression sharpened, but the officer only nodded as if Brooke had supplied evidence for his point.

"Then everybody probably needs a quiet night."

"That's it?"

"Do you need medical help?"

"No."

"Is anybody threatening you?"

Claire stood close enough that her shoulder pressed Brooke's. She looked small and tearful and harmless. "I just don't want to walk alone."

The officer smiled at her. "That's understandable."

Brooke considered showing him the photograph of the broken handle. It would prove a door had broken. It would not prove why.

"Walk home with your sister," he said. "Give your mom one less thing to worry about."

Claire released the backpack as soon as they were a block away. She did not need the strap anymore. She had been given Brooke's company by an adult wearing a badge.

"You didn't have to tell him about the bathroom," Claire said.

"You didn't have to break it."

"I was sorry."

"You didn't say it."

Claire thought about that. "I was bleeding."

Brooke turned toward her. "Is that what sorry means now?"

Claire did not answer. They walked the rest of the way with only an arm's length between them. It was more distance than Claire wanted and less than Brooke needed, which was beginning to feel like the only kind of compromise left.

That evening, Brooke opened the old family video.

Mark's laughter shook the frame. Sarah's shadow crossed the sand. Claire knelt in a purple swimsuit with wet hair pasted to her face, packing both hands around Brooke's knees.

"Don't move," six-year-old Claire ordered.

"I can't feel my feet."

"That's because you live here now."

The camera dipped while Mark laughed. The tide came in and collapsed one side of the mound. Claire shouted at it, then began rebuilding before the water had gone out.

Brooke paused on Claire's face. There was nothing hidden there. No reflective flicker. No second expression. Just a little girl taking an impossible job seriously because she loved the person she was trying to keep.

Brooke opened the bottom drawer of her desk and moved aside a stack of old notebooks. The journal was beneath them, its blue cloth cover faded along the spine. She had not written in it regularly since she started filming. Near the middle, tucked between entries about school and Sarah's rules, she found the beach.

Her ten-year-old handwriting leaned hard to the right.

*Claire buried me and said I had to stay forever. I let her because she looked so happy. We put all the shells back after.*

Brooke read it twice. The memory in the journal was simpler than the video and simpler than what had happened in the library. Claire had always wanted her to stay. Claire had always held on too long when she was afraid. The thing inside that familiarity had not invented those impulses. It had taken them seriously.

She returned the journal to the drawer and opened her list.

Under *THINGS I HAVE SEEN*, she wrote:

*She cried and didn't know why.*

Then:

*She remembered the beach.*

Brooke kept her fingers above the keys. She wanted to write that Claire was still inside. She wanted to write that Claire was gone. Both sentences claimed more than she knew.

She wrote:

*Whatever happened to her can use what she remembers. That doesn't mean the memories are empty.*

Across the hall, Claire opened her bedroom door.

Brooke closed the laptop but left the old video paused on the screen. Claire appeared in the doorway wearing Brooke's flannel, her face clean and composed.

"Can I watch?"

Brooke looked at the little girl frozen in the sand and then at the girl in the doorway.

"From the chair," she said.

Claire accepted the boundary. For that night, at least, she sat where Brooke told her and watched herself rebuild the same collapsing wall until Mark lowered the camera.
