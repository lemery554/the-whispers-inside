# Prologue: The Color

The house had gone quiet the way it only did after midnight, when the refrigerator's hum became the loudest thing in Ashcroft and the streetlight outside Brooke's window stopped competing with anything. She liked it. Quiet was just another kind of light — you could see what was actually there.

She had three windows open on her monitor and a fourth she kept telling herself she'd close.

The footage wasn't hers. That was the first strange thing, if she was being honest, which she tried to be at least with the timeline in front of her: she almost never watched other people's video. Other people shot everything wrong. They held the phone vertical, they zoomed with their feet, they narrated over the exact moment that mattered. But for two days now she had been pulling clips off the internet the way she used to pull frames from a movie she loved, scrubbing back and forth, looking for the seam where the thing became true.

The thing didn't have a name yet. People were calling it *the stomach flu,* or *whatever's going around,* or, in one comment she'd screenshotted without deciding why, *the color.*

She pressed play on the oldest clip she'd saved.

Dispatch audio, thirty-one seconds, uploaded by someone who scanned the local frequencies for a hobby and posted them like other people posted birdsong. A woman's voice, flat and trained, reading an address two towns over. Behind it, faint, another voice — the caller — saying *she can't keep anything down, she can't keep the medicine down, it's coming right back up.* The dispatcher asked what the medicine was. A pause. *The red one,* the caller said. *They gave us the red one.*

Brooke had listened to that clip maybe forty times. She still didn't know why *the red one* raised the hair on her arms and *she can't keep anything down* didn't. Fear was like that. It picked.

She dragged the audio onto her timeline and kept it. She kept everything. It was a reflex she'd stopped apologizing for around the time she was twelve and lost four months of footage to a dead drive and cried about it in a way that had frightened her more than the loss did. Memory was a liar. Memory softened people and sharpened arguments and moved the furniture around when you weren't looking. Video didn't. Video just sat there being exactly what had happened, waiting for you to be brave enough to watch it again.

The next window was the hospital.

It had been shot from a parking structure, one story up, through a stairwell's chain-link, so the whole frame was cut into little diamonds. Below, the emergency entrance of somewhere large — a city, not Ashcroft, the letters on the building too far to read. Ambulances stacked three deep with their back doors open and nobody closing them. A man in scrubs came out through the sliding doors and stood in the ambulance bay with his hands on his knees like a runner, and then he straightened up and went back in, and Brooke understood that he hadn't come out to be sick or to cry. He'd come out to be somewhere that wasn't in there for eleven seconds. She'd counted. She counted things now.

Then the doors opened again and the light from inside fell across the pavement, and the pavement was red.

Not blood-red. That was the part she kept circling. She had seen blood on video — everyone had, that was just the internet — and blood went brown fast, blood dried to rust and shadow. This was still bright. It ran from the doors in a long tongue and pooled at the low point of the bay and it stayed the color of something that wanted to be seen, a red like a warning label, a red like the inside of a throat.

A woman slipped in it. Went down hard, caught herself on an ambulance bumper, and her hand came up printed with it to the wrist, and she looked at her hand for a second — Brooke rewound, watched the second again — and then she just wiped it on her scrubs and kept moving, because there was nowhere in the frame that wasn't the same emergency.

The medicine. It had to be the medicine. Somebody in the comments, a nurse or a liar claiming to be one, said they'd been given cases of it, red liquid in sealed cups, and that the patients threw it up almost the instant it went down, and that at some point the staff had stopped mopping the walls because the walls weren't the emergency, and you couldn't keep cleaning a thing while it was still happening.

Brooke muted the clip. She didn't need the sound of it. She'd learned that about herself editing other people's weddings for gas money: you could always tell the truth of a room with the sound off. Bodies didn't know how to lie the way voices did.

With the sound off she noticed the timing.

She hadn't, before. She pulled two more clips — different city, different night, a phone propped on a vending machine so the frame never moved — and lined them up on the timeline underneath the first, and scrubbed all three at once, and there it was, the thing her eye had been snagging on without telling her: the vomiting came in waves. Not one patient, then another, then another, the ragged way sickness moved through a room. In waves. A dozen people in a hallway would go still, and then together, near enough to together that it made her mouth go dry, they'd fold at the same moment, like something had been said that only they could hear.

She sat back. Her chair complained. Down the hall, Claire's white-noise machine breathed its ocean at nobody, and closer, at the foot of the stairs, she heard Cooper resettle with a groan, the tags on his collar clicking once. Ordinary sounds. The sounds of a house where everyone was fine.

She almost closed the fourth window then. That was the honest thing to admit, later, when she went looking through her own footage for the moment she should have understood something and hadn't. She almost went to bed. She was seventeen and it was a school night and the story on the actual news — she checked, she made herself check — was calm. The lower third of the broadcast said the word *outbreak* in the same font it used for weather. An official Brooke would never see again said the situation was *serious but responding to treatment.* Behind him, out of focus, a hallway that did not look like it was responding to anything.

That was the contradiction she couldn't put down. Not the red. The red was horrible but the red was at least *consistent* — every clip agreed about the red. It was that the calm and the horror were laid over the exact same night and nobody in the calm seemed to have watched the horror, and Brooke's whole self, the part of her that had trusted a lens since before she could spell, revolted at it. Two sources. One night. They could not both be the room.

So she opened the fifth window, the one she'd been saving, and watched the thing that had actually kept her up.

It was a recovery.

Somebody's daughter, the caption said, filmed on a phone from a doorway. A girl a little younger than Brooke, propped up in a hospital bed, and you could see on her — in the gray of her lips, the sunk of her eyes, the IV taped to a hand gone the color of paper — that a few hours ago she had been one of the ones in the hallway. And now she was awake. She was drinking water and keeping it down. She turned her head toward the doorway, toward whoever held the phone, and she smiled, and off camera a woman started to cry the good way, the relieved way, and said, *There she is. There's my girl.*

Everyone in the comments said it was a miracle. Everyone said *thank God.* Everyone said the medicine must be working after all, look at her, look how she's asking for her family.

Brooke watched it four times and could not find the miracle.

She wanted to. She was not a person who enjoyed being the only one in the room who was cold. But she kept landing on the girl's face at the moment she turned toward the door, and something in it was off by a degree she didn't have the vocabulary for yet — a smile that arrived a half-beat too complete, aimed not quite at the person holding the phone but at the *fact* of them, at the doorway itself, at the not-being-alone. It was the face of someone who had wanted one thing so badly for so long that getting it looked less like joy than like a debt being paid.

*You're reading into it,* she told herself, in Sarah's voice, which was the voice she used on herself when she wanted to stop. *You're tired and it's a sad video and you're making it into a scary one because scary is more interesting than sad.*

Maybe. Probably. She dragged the recovery clip onto the timeline anyway, at the end, after the red.

Then she did the thing she would think about for the rest of her life without ever being able to explain it, which was that she named the file.

She could have called it anything. Junk, or refs, or the date. Instead she made a new folder on the drive that mattered, the one she backed up twice, and she sat with her fingers on the keys for a second in the streetlight, and she called it *what happened.*

Not *what's happening.* Past tense. She noticed that only after she'd typed it, and she didn't fix it, because fixing it would have meant deciding it was wrong, and she wasn't sure it was.

She looked at the timeline one more time, all of it laid end to end — the flat dispatcher, the red tongue across the pavement, the dozen people folding at a word she couldn't hear, the girl smiling at a doorway like it had saved her life. A beginning of something. She was good at beginnings; she could feel them the way you feel a note before it's played. Whatever this was, it was worth keeping. It was worth being the person who could say, later, *no, I have it, I have exactly what happened, we don't have to argue about it.*

She saved everything. She checked that her phone was charging — it was, it always was, she'd learned that lesson too. Then she closed the laptop, and the room went dark except for the streetlight, and in the dark she found she was listening. To the refrigerator. To the ocean machine. To Cooper, at the bottom of the stairs, who was awake now, she could tell, not clicking his tags, not sighing, just lying there in the front hall with his head up, pointed at the door, the way he got when a car he didn't know slowed down outside.

There was no car. She listened until she was sure of it. No car, no engine, nothing but the town being asleep the way towns were supposed to be.

She told herself the dog was dreaming.

She went to bed telling herself a lot of things, and most of them were true, and the one that wasn't — the one where none of this had anything to do with her, with her house, with the small warm ordinary people breathing down the hall — she told herself best of all.
