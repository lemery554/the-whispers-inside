# Chapter 6: Red

Saint Vincent's had turned its visitor parking lot into a waiting room.

Reflective cones divided the entrance lanes. Staff in yellow gowns moved between stopped cars, asking questions through cracked windows and tying paper bands around wrists. Beyond them, white screens enclosed the ambulance bay Brooke had seen in the news photograph. Rain had darkened their lower edges.

Sarah lowered her window when a nurse reached them. "Thirteen years old. Fever, vomiting, diarrhea, confusion. She can't keep water down."

The nurse aimed a flashlight through the back window. Claire flinched and buried her face against Brooke's stomach.

"When did she last urinate?"

Sarah looked at Brooke. Neither knew.

The answer moved them out of the car line and into a wheelchair.

Inside, the emergency department remained recognizably a hospital. A registration clerk asked for insurance. A triage nurse checked Claire's pulse, blood pressure, temperature, and oxygen level while another worker wiped the equipment between patients. Claire received an emesis bag, a mask she immediately pulled beneath her nose, and a printed label warning of contact precautions.

The triage nurse asked Claire to rate her pain from zero to ten. Claire said four. Sarah said eight at the same time.

"Her pain or your worry?" the nurse asked without looking up.

Sarah almost smiled. "Both."

The nurse pressed a fingernail until the color left it and counted the seconds until it returned. She asked Claire to stick out her tongue, then noted dry mucous membranes and delayed capillary refill in the chart. When Claire stood for the scale, her knees softened. The nurse caught her elbow and changed something on the screen.

Brooke watched the order of questions. The staff were not guessing. Each answer moved Claire through a system that had worked before tonight. The horror was not that nobody knew what to do. It was that the same competent sequence was being asked to hold too many people at once.

Competence survived everywhere. There was simply too much required of it.

Patients filled the waiting-room chairs and the corridor beyond them. Plastic basins sat beneath seats. A custodian changed gloves beside a yellow spill sign while a second stain spread near the vending machines. Gurneys lined one wall, each separated by a curtain that stopped several feet short of privacy.

A nurse told Sarah that one family member could remain past triage.

"I'm staying," Sarah said.

Claire caught Brooke's sleeve. "No. Both."

"I need you to wait here," Sarah told Brooke. "Keep your phone on."

Claire tightened her grip. "She comes."

The nurse crouched until they were nearly eye level. "We have to limit how many people go back. Your mom will stay with you."

"Brooke too."

Her voice had no force left, but she did not release the sleeve. Sarah peeled Claire's fingers away one at a time.

"I'll be right here," Brooke said.

Claire stared at her as the wheelchair moved through the double doors. The look remained fixed until the doors met between them.

Brooke sat beneath a television with captions turned on. A local anchor reported that most patients were improving rapidly with supportive treatment. Behind him, National Guard members unloaded cardboard cases at a state distribution center.

Registration gave her a paper visitor slip that did not permit entry and directed her to a vending area already emptied of water. She bought ginger ale because it was the only clear drink left, then did not open it. Around her, people conducted private emergencies at public volume.

A father argued that his wife could not understand English well enough to go back alone. An older man slept upright with both hands around a plastic basin. Two teenage boys shared one charging cable while reading updates from somebody behind the treatment doors. Every few minutes, a staff member called a name and several families stood before realizing it was not theirs.

Brooke opened her camera but did not record faces. She filmed the television caption saying *MOST PATIENTS RECOVER* above a row of shoes stained red near the vending machines. Then she lowered the phone and wrote the time in her notes.

At 12:18 a.m., Sarah texted that Claire had an IV and bloodwork pending. At 12:41, she wrote that the fever was 103.1. At 1:06, a nurse brought Brooke through because Claire had become too agitated for Sarah to calm alone.

The nurse made Brooke sanitize her hands, put on a yellow gown, and repeat that she would stay inside the marked treatment area. Brooke tied the gown badly. The nurse retied it without comment while walking.

They passed rooms whose doors had been propped open for visibility. In one, a woman argued that she had already recovered and wanted to leave with her husband. In another, three family members stood pressed together around an empty bed while a transport worker waited with a wheelchair. A physician at a computer dictated that a patient was alert, oriented, ambulatory, and refusing separation from family. The language sounded clinical enough to make the last phrase seem ordinary.

Near the pediatric bay, cases of the red formulation had been stacked against a wall beneath a paper sign reading *ORAL REHYDRATION SUPPORT*. Several boxes were wet along the bottom. A supply worker moved the dry cases onto a cart and left the stained cardboard where it was.

The treatment space had once held six beds. It now held ten. Claire lay beneath a thin blanket with an IV taped to her hand. Her lips had split at the center. Sarah sat beside her, one palm resting on Claire's ankle as if contact could keep her in place.

An IV pump alarmed behind one curtain until a nurse silenced it with the heel of her hand while adjusting another patient's oxygen. A laminated sign taped to the medication station listed contact precautions, fluid-replacement thresholds, and neurological checks every hour. Somebody had added *DOCUMENT ORAL INTAKE* in red marker.

Claire's first bag of saline was already half empty. A second smaller bag hung beside it, and the nurse explained that it contained potassium because vomiting and diarrhea had lowered Claire's level enough to affect muscle and heart function.

"Is her heart okay?" Sarah asked.

"Her rhythm is okay. We're correcting it before it becomes a problem."

The nurse attached leads to Claire's chest anyway. Claire complained that the adhesive pulled. Sarah called that a good complaint because it sounded like her.

"They gave her nausea medicine," Sarah said. "The normal kind."

The distinction made Brooke look toward the end of the bay.

A cart stood beside the medication station, loaded with sealed white cups. Through their translucent sides, the liquid appeared almost luminous red. A nurse peeled back lids and distributed them with printed instruction sheets.

"What is that?" Brooke asked.

Sarah shook her head. "Something they're using for vomiting."

The doctor arrived before Brooke could ask more. Dr. Shah looked younger than Sarah and more tired than anyone Brooke had ever seen standing upright. He said Claire's labs showed significant dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. They were admitting her to pediatric observation for IV fluids, neurological checks, and the oral treatment being used under emergency guidance.

He washed his hands while speaking, twenty seconds by the wall clock, then repeated the neurological questions the nurse had asked. Claire knew her name and the hospital but called Saturday Friday. Dr. Shah did not correct her immediately. He asked whether school had been open that day, whether anyone else at home was ill, whether Claire had eaten anything unusual, traveled, taken antibiotics, or been near untreated water.

Sarah answered until Brooke interrupted to mention Cooper's behavior. Dr. Shah looked at her for one second, neither dismissive nor interested enough to record it.

"Animals can react to fever, smell, stress, and changes in household behavior," he said.

It was a useful answer that explained nothing Brooke wanted explained.

"What is it called?" Brooke asked.

Dr. Shah pointed to the emergency-formulation code printed on the instruction sheet. It looked more like inventory than a name. "We don't have evidence that it treats the cause. The goal is to help patients tolerate fluids."

"Is it working?"

He looked toward the crowded bay. "Some people are improving."

That was not the same answer.

Sarah read the emergency-use sheet from top to bottom. It listed the formulation code, ingredients Brooke recognized from electrolyte drinks, a flavoring agent, and several words she did not. Under *Known limitations*, it said the product had not been established to shorten the underlying illness.

"Then why give it?" Brooke asked.

Dr. Shah had already moved to the next bed. The nurse answered while checking Claire's IV. "Because getting fluid into people by mouth means we can save IV supplies for patients who need them most."

"But they're throwing it up."

The nurse looked toward the medication cart. "Some are."

She did not sound convinced by the distinction.

Brooke started recording after he left. She held her phone low, framing only the medication cart and the clock above it. One patient drank. Then another. Across the bay, a child began crying that it tasted like pennies.

Claire accepted her cup because Sarah asked her to. The liquid stained her lower lip red.

"Small sip," the nurse said.

Claire swallowed once. Her whole body tightened.

Three beds away, a man folded forward. The sound of liquid hitting the floor came a second before two other patients began retching. Claire shoved the cup away and turned toward the basin.

The red came back brighter than it had gone in.

It splashed the basin, Sarah's gown, the white sheet, and the floor beneath the bed. Another patient vomited across the curtain. Shoes squeaked as staff moved between them. A nurse slipped, caught the medication cart, and sent sealed cups rolling in red water.

For several seconds the room seemed to move together. Bodies contracted. Curtains shook. Staff called for bags, suction, clean gowns, more towels. The custodian from the waiting room appeared with a fresh mop head, stopped at the bay entrance, and looked as if somebody had asked him to empty the ocean.

One nurse began counting aloud, not patients but seconds between episodes. Another called the medication station and asked whether the batch number matched the previous shipment. The answer was lost beneath retching and alarms.

A respiratory therapist moved a child onto his side and cleared his mouth with suction. An aide guided a confused woman back onto her bed before she stepped barefoot into the spill. Nobody ran. Running would have made the room less usable. They moved quickly inside narrow lanes that existed only because each worker trusted the others to keep theirs.

Brooke had expected panic to look disorganized. This was worse. It had protocols.

Brooke kept recording until Sarah saw the phone.

"Put it away."

"They said it was supposed to help."

"Put it away and help me."

Brooke did. She held Claire's hair while Sarah changed the sheet beneath her. The nurse replaced the IV tubing that had pulled loose. Red footprints traveled outward from the bed and remained after the visible spill was gone.

At three, Claire slept.

Brooke sat in the plastic chair with Claire's damp socks sealed in a belongings bag beneath her feet. Sarah had changed into a clean disposable gown, but red remained in the seam of one thumbnail. She scraped at it until the skin around the nail reddened too.

"Stop," Brooke said.

Sarah tucked the hand beneath her opposite arm. "I can still smell it."

"It's in the room."

"I know."

They spoke softly because Claire was sleeping and because neither wanted to restart the workshop argument in a place where other families could hear. Sarah leaned her head against the wall.

"I'm sorry," Brooke said.

Sarah opened her eyes. "For what?"

There were too many possible answers. The sentence about being kept. The camera. The fact that part of Brooke had noticed the red medicine as footage while Claire was vomiting it.

"Yesterday," she said.

Sarah looked at Claire. "Not here."

It was not forgiveness. It was a promise that the argument still belonged to a future in which they went home.

At three-thirty, the lights dimmed by a degree that would have been comforting in any room without ten beds. The nurses continued neurological checks every hour. Claire woke when they asked her name, answered, and immediately searched for Sarah's hand. Across the bay, the woman who had held her husband's sleeve earlier was awake again. Her husband slept folded over a chair, his forehead against the mattress. She watched him without blinking until an aide crossed between them.

The aide blocked her view for less than two seconds. The woman pushed herself upright, pulse monitor alarming, and relaxed only after the aide moved on.

Brooke wrote *fear of separation after recovery?* in her notes, then added a question mark large enough to make the phrase look embarrassed.

At four-ten, Claire kept down an ounce of water. Sarah cried quietly and laughed at herself for crying. Brooke filmed only the measurement marks on the cup and Sarah's hand holding it. Claire's face remained outside the frame.

Her fever fell to 101.7. She kept down two ice chips, then four. By four, she answered her name, the hospital, and the day correctly. Around them, other patients improved with the same improbable speed.

A woman across the aisle sat upright after hours beneath a blanket. When an orderly arrived to take her for imaging, she hooked two fingers through her husband's sleeve. He kissed her knuckles and promised he would wait. She kept hold of the fabric until the orderly gently separated them.

Before dawn, Mark called. Brooke went into the corridor so the ringing would not wake Claire.

His video showed darkness and part of a dashboard. "How is she?"

"Better. Maybe. They gave her the red medicine. She threw it up. Everybody did."

The image froze. His voice continued in broken pieces.

"Leaving now... road closure... hear me?"

"Where are you?"

"Still near... Brooke, tell your mom..."

The call failed.

She called back twice. Both attempts went to voicemail.

At dawn, the walls near Claire's bed had been wiped but not cleaned. Pale red streaks remained in the grout. A nurse stepped through them while carrying discharge papers for a patient who had been unable to stand three hours earlier.

A hospitalist from pediatric observation reviewed Claire's chart and explained that they would continue IV fluids, repeat electrolytes, monitor urine output, and advance her diet only after she tolerated clear liquids. Sarah asked whether Claire remained contagious. The doctor said they should assume she was until the health department gave different guidance.

"How long after the vomiting stops?" Sarah asked.

"We don't know yet."

"Should her sister stay away from her?"

The hospitalist looked at Brooke, then at the crowded bay where separation had become mostly theoretical. "At home, use separate towels, clean high-touch surfaces, and limit close contact where you can."

Claire woke during the answer. Her hand moved across the sheet until she found Brooke's wrist.

"You're here," she said.

"Yeah."

Her grip was weak, ordinary, and familiar. Brooke let her keep it.

Claire would remain in pediatric observation through Sunday.
