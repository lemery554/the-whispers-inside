# Chapter 23

## Pack Light

Brooke learned how little of her life fit inside a backpack.

Two shirts. Underwear. Socks. Toothbrush. Charger. Camera batteries. The camera itself wrapped in a sweatshirt Claire had not stolen. Cooper's collapsible water bowl. Cash from the envelope behind her editing manuals. Mark's small flashlight from the garage.

She packed everything beneath a layer of schoolbooks and left the bag where it always sat beside the desk.

The obvious things were too dangerous to take. The framed beach photograph would leave a clean rectangle in the dust. The keepsake box was locked and Sarah had the key. Brooke could not take every hard drive without Sarah noticing the empty shelf.

She copied the chemistry folder, observation list, hospital footage, and family recordings onto one external drive. The progress bar crawled while Claire moved around in the room across the hall.

When it finished, Brooke disconnected the drive and tested it on the old laptop beneath her bed. The folder opened. The hospital clips played. Mark's final voicemail still contained the single word that might have meant home.

She renamed nothing. Descriptive filenames could become a map for whoever opened the drive. Instead she created a plain text inventory on the old laptop, printed one copy, and deleted the document. The paper went beneath the lining of the camera bag.

Her phone held the diner address, Anna's messages, and every current contact. Brooke turned off location sharing with Sarah, then discovered the family safety app would notify Sarah of the change. She left it on.

Instead she disabled mobile data and practiced turning it back on from the lock screen. The map to the diner was simple enough to remember. She would use airplane mode after leaving and accept that the gap itself would tell Sarah she had gone.

There was no invisible way out of a family that monitored her for love.

Sarah had decided they were all staying home again.

"The school can manage without you for one day," she had said.

"Yesterday you wanted routine."

"Yesterday a reporter was outside."

"Was he there today?"

"I don't know."

It was the first answer Sarah had given in days that sounded like an admission instead of a door closing. Brooke almost trusted it.

She checked Anna's email again.

Brooke had replied at 1:13 that morning:

*What question?*

Anna answered at 1:19.

*The one about who looked for you.*

Brooke had stared at those words until the screen dimmed.

She wrote:

*Who are you?*

Anna replied:

*Someone who watched every second you asked people to watch.*

A second message arrived a minute later.

*That sounded less strange before I sent it. I mean your public videos.*

True, if she meant the videos. Intimate anyway.

Brooke asked why the diner.

*It is public. You can sit near the door. You can leave whenever you want.*

No heart. No *please*. No attempt to make refusal cruel.

Brooke had not answered again.

At noon she took Cooper into her room and closed the door. He settled on the rug without scratching to leave. Across the hall, Claire's floorboards creaked once, then stopped.

Brooke opened the window.

Cold air pushed the curtain inward. The porch roof sat below at a shallow angle, dark shingles damp from the morning mist. She had climbed onto it once when she was twelve because Claire had locked them out of the upstairs bathroom and Brooke wanted to prove the windows connected.

Mark had caught her halfway across.

*If you break your neck, your mother will kill me before the fall does,* he had said.

He had shown her where to place her feet while helping her back through the window.

Brooke set one boot on the sill. Cooper stood immediately.

"Stay."

He whined.

"I'm checking."

She leaned out far enough to see the porch edge and the maple tree beyond it. The garage roof over the rear addition was lower, and its slope ended near the wooden trellis. Cooper could not follow either route without help.

A metallic scrape sounded beneath her.

She froze with one hand on the sill.

The sound came again from the garage. A heavy latch, then metal sliding over metal.

Mark's gun safe.

Brooke stepped back inside.

Sarah appeared in the driveway below carrying a box of old installer paperwork from the garage. She set it on the hood of the car and began sorting. Beyond her, the safe remained bolted beside the file cabinet, exactly where Mark had installed it.

The sound had been the metal file cabinet beside the safe, not the safe itself.

Brooke closed the window. Her pulse did not slow.

She added the route to the observation file, then deleted the entry. If Sarah searched the laptop, Brooke would be writing her own instructions for capture.

Instead she recorded the path with her camera, pretending to test exposure. Window latch. Roof angle. Trellis. Fence gate. Back road.

She filmed Cooper sitting beside the backpack.

"This is not a plan," she whispered.

His ears lifted.

"It's evidence that I had one."

She stopped recording.

Brooke checked Cooper's harness and packed the collapsible bowl beside two sealed water bottles. He watched every item disappear into the bag and began panting although the room was cold.

"We're not going anywhere," Brooke told him.

His ears moved toward the door.

The lie sounded worse aloud. Brooke crouched and pressed her forehead to his. "I don't know where we're going."

Cooper licked her chin and sneezed.

She added a small towel, antiseptic wipes, and the roll of gauze Sarah kept in the upstairs bathroom. The items made the backpack heavier and the plan more real.

Then she watched the clip once, understood that it was a map for anyone who found the camera, and deleted it. She emptied the camera's trash folder too. The route would have to stay in her head.

At three, Sarah returned the tiny brass key.

She placed it on Brooke's dresser. "I shouldn't have taken this."

Brooke looked from the key to her mother. "Why are you giving it back?"

"Because it belongs to you."

"That's not an answer."

"It is the answer."

Sarah remained by the doorway, not entering farther. Her apology looked sincere. It was also useful. Both things could be true.

"The keypad company is sending someone Monday morning," Sarah said.

It was Friday.

"We can't wait until Monday."

"We are home all weekend."

"That's what I'm afraid of."

Sarah's expression shifted, but she did not reach for her. "I know you believe that."

She left the door open behind her.

Brooke locked the keepsake box and put the key in the backpack.

The original letter was still inside. She considered taking it, then imagined Sarah finding the empty envelope. She photographed every page instead. The handwriting looked more personal through the camera than it did in her hands.

The woman who wrote it had said she hoped Brooke would grow up somewhere safe. She had not promised to return. She had not asked to be forgiven. The letter was an explanation shaped like an absence.

Brooke opened a message to Mark.

*I don't know if you're getting these. Mom and Claire are both wrong. Someone came into the house. My journal is gone. If you are still you, call me and don't ask Mom first.*

She did not send it. If Mark had changed, the message told him she planned to leave. If he had not, it might be the last cruel thing she ever sent him.

She saved it as a draft.

Then she recorded a message instead.

"Dad, if you get this later, I left because I couldn't tell what was safe anymore. Mom and Claire remember everything, but they don't hear no the same way. Someone used the front-door code. My journal is missing. I have Cooper and the camera."

She stopped, deleted it, and began again without diagnosing anyone.

"Dad, I'm leaving the house tonight. I'm taking Cooper. The evidence drive is with me. I tried calling you."

That version sounded like a note police would play after something terrible happened. She deleted it too.

In the end, she left the unsent text exactly as it was. Uncertainty was not a reason to leave no record. It was also not permission to tell a possibly changed father how to find her.

At dinner Sarah served roasted vegetables over rice.

The news played quietly from the living room. A banner advised residents to remain with household members through the weekend because temporary service disruptions could delay transportation and emergency response. Ashcroft's emergency order also placed minors under an eleven o'clock curfew while staffing remained reduced.

"Convenient," Brooke said.

Sarah put down her fork. "It is an advisory."

"It tells everybody to stay home."

"It says emergency response may be delayed."

Claire looked between them. "We could watch the beach videos tonight."

Brooke's fork stopped.

"Why those?"

"They make you less mad."

"No, they make me sad."

Claire considered this. "Sometimes those look the same."

Sarah reached for Claire's hand. Claire took it.

Brooke went upstairs before dinner was finished.

At eight forty, Anna sent one more message.

*If you do not come, I will understand.*

Brooke waited for a second line. Nothing arrived.

She imagined the diner: glass windows, two exits, people at nearby tables. She imagined a woman who knew the real name at the bottom of the letter. She imagined Sarah discovering the backpack before Brooke learned whether the only person offering her a choice was real.

At nine, Brooke put Cooper's leash beneath the top flap of the bag. She placed shoes beside the window and loosened the screen.

Cooper lay down on her side of the closed bedroom door.

Claire stopped in the hall.

"Good night," she said.

Brooke did not answer.

After a moment, Claire went away.

Brooke rested her hand against the door above Cooper's head and listened to the house she planned to leave.

Downstairs, the television anchor repeated the weekend advisory. Sarah lowered the volume. Claire asked whether Brooke was still angry.

"She needs time," Sarah said.

"How much?"

"Let her choose."

The phrase was soft enough that Brooke might have imagined it through the floor and walls.

Claire answered, "Okay."

Neither came upstairs.

Brooke sat beside the packed bag and understood that space could become another way of guiding her. She checked Anna's last message again.

*If you do not come, I will understand.*

Every person in the house had finally learned to wait.
