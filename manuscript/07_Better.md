# Chapter 7: Better

Monday morning, Claire walked out of Saint Vincent's under her own power.

Sarah carried the discharge folder, two plastic bags, and the gray blanket. Brooke carried everything else because nobody trusted Claire with anything that could spill. The morning sun made them all squint after the hospital's flat white light.

"I feel disgusting," Claire said.

Her voice sounded like hers again.

The discharge nurse had called the improvement typical. Continue fluids. Offer bland foods. Return for confusion, persistent vomiting, or inability to drink. Follow up with the pediatrician. The printed page described a gastrointestinal illness and did not mention the red treatment beyond a handwritten time.

At home, the kitchen smelled faintly of coffee left in the pot and the lavender cleaner Sarah used on the counters. Cooper waited behind the gate. His tail moved when Sarah entered, faster when he saw Brooke, then stopped when Claire stepped through.

Claire dropped to her knees. "I survived."

Cooper remained near the refrigerator.

"Come here."

He looked toward Brooke.

Sarah unlatched the gate. "Give him a minute. You smell like the hospital."

Claire accepted the explanation. Brooke watched Cooper retreat when she stood.

By noon, Claire's fever was gone. She showered, changed into Brooke's blue hoodie without asking, and ate half a bowl of rice. Sarah slept on the living-room sofa with one hand still curled around her phone.

The house had the stale, abandoned feeling of a place they had left in a hurry. A glass of water still stood on the stairs where Sarah had set it before deciding Claire needed the hospital. Brooke found one of Claire's socks beneath the kitchen table and the other beside the downstairs bathroom. Cooper followed her while she opened curtains and emptied cups, keeping close until Claire came into the room. Then he chose a doorway and watched.

Brooke tried to photograph the normal things. Claire's sneakers kicked beneath the radiator. Sarah asleep with her mouth slightly open. The discharge folder on the counter beneath a grocery coupon. She told herself the images were for the part afterward, when this became the weekend Claire had a terrible stomach virus and everybody complained about the hospital for years.

Claire caught her pointing the camera toward the sofa.

"Mom will murder you if you post her sleeping."

"I'm not posting it."

"Then why take it?"

Brooke lowered the camera. Before the hospital, Claire would have leaned across the screen and demanded approval rights over every picture in which even one strand of her hair appeared. Now she only waited.

"Because this is what today looks like," Brooke said.

"It looks boring."

"That's the dream."

Claire smiled. It was quick, crooked, and familiar enough that Brooke felt foolish for every second she had spent watching Cooper.

Brooke sat at the kitchen table reviewing the hospital clips with the sound off. The red treatment cart. The cups. Claire's stained mouth. The room contracting almost together. She cropped out the other patients and the staff name badges, then chose a close frame of the emergency-formulation code. She cut away before Claire vomited, restored the section, and hated herself a little for deciding that removing it changed what happened.

When Claire entered, Brooke minimized the window.

"Were you filming me?"

"The hospital."

"I was at the hospital."

"You were barely in it."

Claire opened the refrigerator. "Do we have mushrooms?"

Brooke looked up. "What?"

"Mushrooms."

"We have never had mushrooms because you describe them as wet ears."

"I did not say wet ears."

"You said boiled ears. Sorry."

Claire searched the vegetable drawer and found a carton Sarah had bought for an omelet. She ate one slice raw.

Brooke waited for the face Claire always made when a mushroom touched her food. It did not come.

"That's good," Claire said.

"No, it isn't."

"You don't like them either."

"That isn't the point."

Sarah woke at their voices and came into the kitchen. When Brooke explained, she smiled with exhausted relief. "Her appetite is back. That's good."

"She hates mushrooms."

"People's tastes change."

"Since yesterday?"

Sarah's smile disappeared. "She almost had to be admitted. Can we let one thing be good?"

Claire stood between them chewing a second slice.

Brooke said yes because Sarah needed it and because Claire was upright, hydrated, and rolling her eyes again. She helped make eggs with mushrooms while Cooper watched from the hall.

Sarah called Mark while the eggs cooked. The call failed twice before connecting with his face broken into squares by the hotel Wi-Fi. He was still in western Pennsylvania, where a job-site closure had stranded his crew behind a county travel advisory. He asked Claire to hold the phone closer, then farther away, then laughed when she complained that he was directing her like one of Brooke's videos.

"Look at you," he said. "Back among the living."

Sarah closed her eyes at the phrase.

"Too soon?" Mark asked.

"A lot too soon."

Claire told him about the red medicine and left out nearly everything else. She made the hospital sound gross rather than frightening. Mark promised to drive home as soon as the site released the company trucks. Sarah asked whether he had eaten. Claire moved beside Brooke during the answer and leaned against her until their shoulders touched.

On the small screen, Mark noticed. "Taking care of your sister?"

"She's taking care of me," Claire said.

Brooke made herself smile. The connection froze on Mark smiling back.

When the picture moved again, his attention had shifted toward someone outside the frame. A voice called his name. Mark said he had to go and that he loved them. Claire answered first and loudest. After the call ended, Sarah remained at the stove with the dark phone in her hand.

"He'll be home soon," she said.

Nobody had asked.

That afternoon, Brooke assembled her second upload. She titled it *My sister got sick*. She included Claire's fever timeline, the hospital notices, the red cups, and the recovery instructions. She did not include the bathroom, Claire crying about Cooper, or the cruel line from two days before. Those belonged to Claire, not the outbreak.

She recorded one final sentence.

"She's home now. Her fever is gone. The hospital says most people recover quickly, and that seems true. I still don't know what the red treatment is supposed to do."

The upload began collecting comments before dinner. The first criticism that stayed with her asked whether Claire had agreed to have the worst night of her life posted online. Brooke began typing that the video might help somebody, then stopped. Claire had not agreed. Brooke closed the page.

She reopened the project instead and watched the cut as if someone else had made it. The hospital employees were unidentifiable. The children in the waiting room appeared only as shoes, blankets, and hands. Claire's face showed for eleven seconds. Brooke had chosen the seconds in which she looked sick enough to prove the story and well enough that posting her did not feel cruel.

That choice bothered Brooke more than the comment.

She carried the laptop to Claire and paused before the first hospital shot. "Watch this."

Claire groaned. "Do I have to?"

"No. That's the point. You can tell me to take it down."

Claire watched without speaking. At the image of the red cup she rubbed her tongue against her teeth. When the video ended, she asked how many people had seen it.

"A hundred and nine."

"That's barely people."

"Claire."

"Leave it up." She reached for the progress bar and replayed the shot of Sarah arguing with the intake clerk. "Mom looks scary there."

"Mom was scary there."

"You didn't show me throwing up."

"I promised."

Claire nodded, apparently satisfied, and leaned her head against Brooke's upper arm. "You can make another one if something else happens."

Brooke looked down at her. "What else would happen?"

Claire's eyes stayed on the frozen hospital frame. "Nothing."

The pediatrician called before dinner. Sarah put the phone on speaker and carried it through the kitchen while checking Claire's temperature for the third time. Dr. Shah asked about fluids, urine, dizziness, abdominal pain, and whether Claire could keep down food. Claire answered each question with impatient precision.

"Any confusion?" Dr. Shah asked.

"No," Sarah said.

Claire said, "Mom asked me the president and my birthday six times."

"Only four."

"It felt like six."

Dr. Shah laughed softly. "That sounds promising. Any unusual behavior?"

Brooke looked at the mushrooms.

Sarah followed her gaze. "She's tired. A little clingy."

"Normal after a frightening hospitalization," Dr. Shah said. "Let her nervous system settle. Keep the next few days quiet and predictable."

Claire took Brooke's hand beneath the counter. Her fingers were cool and loose. Brooke let them stay.

"What about the medicine they gave her?" Brooke asked.

The pause was brief but real. "Which medication?"

"The red liquid. The emergency formula."

"That was part of the hospital protocol. Saint Vincent's can answer questions about the specific formulation."

"The discharge sheet doesn't name it."

"Then call the number on the packet tomorrow. My concern tonight is hydration and return of symptoms."

Sarah thanked her before Brooke could ask again. When the call ended, she said, "You heard her. Quiet and predictable."

"She also didn't answer."

"She is Claire's pediatrician, not the hospital pharmacy."

Claire squeezed Brooke's hand. "I'm okay."

The sentence was exactly what Brooke wanted. It was also what every adult needed Claire to say. Brooke turned her palm upward and squeezed back.

For the rest of the afternoon, recovery performed itself convincingly. Claire complained about the bland-food list. She beat Sarah at a card game and accused her of cheating. She sent Mark a photograph of the mushroom eggs with the caption *I have survived and become sophisticated*. When Sarah made her walk once around the ground floor to check for dizziness, Claire added a runway turn in the foyer.

Brooke filmed none of it.

She wanted to remember without creating evidence. She wanted one hour that did not have to become useful later.

Claire sat cross-legged on the living-room rug watching a cooking competition. A plate beside her held mushrooms and plain crackers.

"Can I sit here?" Brooke asked.

Claire looked surprised. "It's a free country."

Brooke sat on the sofa. Cooper climbed beside her and pressed his full weight against her thigh.

Claire patted the rug. "You too."

He lowered his head onto Brooke's knee.

Sarah returned with three blankets and covered them as if recovery could be secured by warmth. Claire complained that hers was too heavy. Sarah called that a normal complaint and kissed the top of her head.

When the credits began, Claire reached back and rested her hand over Brooke's ankle. She left it there after she fell asleep.
