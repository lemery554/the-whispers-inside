# Chapter 12: Everyone Is Fine

Brooke went back to school because staying home had started to feel like agreeing with the house.

Sarah drove her, pleased about it in a way she did not hide, as though Brooke returning to a normal building for a normal day were a small vote against everything Brooke had been saying. Claire wanted to come to the car. She stood on the front step in the cold in her socks until Sarah told her to go back inside, and she watched them pull out with both hands pressed to the storm door glass, and Brooke made herself look forward and not back at the two pale prints her sister's palms left on the door.

Ashcroft High was open and lit and full, and for the first ten minutes it was so ordinary that Brooke felt the accusation settle back over her. The buses idled. The disinfectant bottles still sat by two classroom doors. A banner for the winter fundraiser sagged over the main hall. People had come back. That was the story the building told, and it was a good story, and Brooke wanted to believe it more than she wanted to be right.

Then she started to see it, and once she started she could not stop, because seeing was the only thing she was good at.

It was in the way people sat. In the library, two girls she was fairly sure did not know each other, one a senior, one a freshman, sat pressed together at a four-person table with the whole rest of the table empty, shoulder to shoulder, hip to hip, sharing nothing, saying nothing, comfortable in a way that people who had just met were not comfortable. Neither of them looked at the other. They did not need to. They sat the way a family sits in a waiting room, close for the sake of closeness, and when the freshman got up for a book the senior turned to keep her in view the entire time she was gone.

In homeroom, Mr. Bell passed around a plastic container of olives. He had spent three years complaining that the smell of olives ruined the faculty refrigerator. Now he ate them between attendance names and offered them to students with an absent generosity. Three people accepted. One was a girl Brooke had once watched remove an olive from nachos with a napkin.

"New health kick?" somebody asked.

Mr. Bell looked at the olive pinched between his fingers as if he had forgotten it was there. "They taste clean."

The room laughed. He laughed too, a little late, and set the container on the desk where anybody could reach it.

In the hall after second period, an assistant principal stood beside the exit telling a boy he could not leave until his mother arrived.

"She told me to walk home," the boy said.

"Families need to know where everyone is right now."

"She knows."

"Then waiting won't hurt."

The assistant principal's hand stayed lightly on the boy's shoulder. Not restraining him. Not quite.

And it was outside the second-floor bathroom, where a girl stood waiting.

Brooke almost walked past her. She was a small girl, maybe a freshman, standing against the wall beside the bathroom door with her backpack still on both shoulders, and she was not on her phone and not doing homework and not doing any of the things people did while they waited. She was just watching the door. When it opened and another girl came out, the waiting girl straightened and fell into step so close behind her that they moved through the hallway as one shape, and Brooke understood that she had been standing outside a bathroom door for however long it takes, waiting, because the person inside had gone somewhere she could not follow, and the waiting had been the whole activity.

Brooke stood at her locker and felt the specific loneliness of being the only person in a building who could see the water rising.

She went to the counselor's office at lunch, not because she had decided to but because her feet took her there while her head was still arguing. Ms. Ferro had been the school counselor since Brooke was a freshman, a kind, tired woman with a candy dish and a poster about feelings, and Brooke thought that if she said it out loud to one adult who was paid to listen, she would find out whether she was crazy.

"I'm glad you came in," Ms. Ferro said, before Brooke had said anything. "It's been a hard couple of weeks. A lot of our recovered individuals are back this week, and that's wonderful, but it can be an adjustment for everybody."

*Recovered individuals.* Brooke had heard the phrase that morning on the news alert Sarah read at the counter, in the same shape, in the same slot in the sentence. Two days ago the word had been *victims.* She had watched it change.

"Some kids seem different," Brooke said carefully. "Since they got better."

"Of course they do." Ms. Ferro smiled. "They've been through something frightening. What they need most right now is reassurance. Closeness. To feel that their people are near." She said *their people are near* like a line she had been given, warm and smooth and slightly too finished, the way you can hear a phone menu underneath a real voice. "The worst thing we can do is make them feel separated after everything. We're all just so glad to have everyone back together."

"Right," Brooke said.

"Are you sleeping okay?"

"Yeah."

"Your sister's home safe. That's the main thing." Ms. Ferro's eyes were kind and did not have the green in them, and her hands were normal on the desk, and she was, Brooke was almost certain, exactly what she had always been, an unchanged woman repeating language that had been fed to her because it sounded like compassion and cost nothing to say. That was the part that frightened Brooke most in the whole building. It did not need everyone to be changed. It only needed the changed to set the words, and the unchanged to find them reasonable.

Brooke took a candy she did not want and left.

She was tired in a way that had gone past her body by the time the last bell rang. She texted Sarah that she wanted to walk Cooper before dinner, which was true, and that she would be home by five, which she intended, and Sarah, delighted by the ordinariness of a girl wanting to walk her dog, said fine and did not argue. Brooke walked home, clipped Cooper's leash on in the front hall while Claire hovered and asked twice to come and was told no by Sarah, and got out of the house with the dog and the leash and the leftover daylight, and for six blocks she felt something that was almost like being a person again.

The small dog park off Linden was nearly empty. A man threw a ball for a spaniel at the far end and did not look up. Brooke let Cooper off the leash inside the fence and sat on the low bench. Cooper put his nose to the ground and became a dog again, snuffling the fence line with his tail up.

"He looks relieved," a woman said.

She was sitting at the other end of the bench. Brooke had not heard her arrive, or she had been there the whole time and Brooke had not registered her. She was somewhere in her forties, dressed plainly and warmly, with a paper pharmacy bag beside her and one glove folded inside the other. She had no dog.

"He's had a weird week," Brooke said, because it was easier than deciding not to answer.

"A lot of us have." The woman watched Cooper work the fence. "He's a good watcher. Or maybe he just likes the fence."

Brooke's attention sharpened by a degree. "Yeah," she said. "He does."

"People don't listen to dogs enough." The woman adjusted the pharmacy bag before the wind could tip it over. "I cut through here from the store sometimes. Quieter than Linden."

Cooper came back across the grass. He had been happy, loose, and as he got closer to the bench Brooke watched him take in the woman. He slowed. His tail came down, not tucked, only lowered, and he stopped a few feet off and looked at her, and his nose worked, and he did not come the rest of the way.

Brooke went cold for a second, because she knew that slowing. She had built a whole document around that slowing.

But it was not the same as Claire. Cooper did not back away. He held his ground and watched the woman, tail low, while she kept her hands to herself. After a moment he sat several feet away, still facing her.

"He doesn't have to come over," the woman said. "He doesn't know me. That's smart."

"He usually likes everybody," Brooke said, and heard herself apologizing for the dog, and did not know why she wanted the woman not to feel rejected.

"He likes you," the woman said. "That's the important one."

They sat. The spaniel and its owner left. The light went from gold to gray at the tops of the trees.

"You make videos," the woman said, and Brooke's whole body went still, and the woman must have felt it, because she went on gently, without turning it into a demand. "I've watched your videos. The ones about the illness. Your sister." She said it plainly, a fact offered and then set down, no question attached to it. "You see things clearly. Most people don't. You looked at what everyone was calling recovery and you noticed it was wrong, and you kept noticing, even when it made you the only one."

It was the thing Brooke had wanted an adult to say to her all day, all week, and it had come from a stranger on a bench who wanted nothing, who had not even asked her a question, who had simply named the true thing and left it there for Brooke to pick up or not. Brooke felt her eyes sting and hated it and looked at the dog.

"Everybody thinks I'm being dramatic," she said.

"You're not being dramatic." The woman said it the way you state the weather. "You're paying attention. It's a lonely thing to do. I'm sorry it's lonely."

Brooke did not say anything for a while. The woman picked at a loose corner of the pharmacy receipt, folded it, and tucked it into the bag.

Cooper got up, finally, and came to Brooke, and pushed his head under her hand, and turned to keep the woman in his sight while he did it. Brooke clipped the leash on. It was almost five.

"I have to get back," Brooke said.

"Of course." The woman pulled on the second glove. "Go carefully. It gets dark fast now."

Brooke was halfway to the gate before she realized she had not gotten the woman's name. She looked back. The woman was still on the bench, hands folded, watching Cooper go with an expression Brooke could not read at that distance and did not try to. She lifted one hand, not a wave exactly, just an acknowledgment. Brooke lifted hers and went out through the gate into the falling dark, feeling lighter than she had in days. After a week of people clinging to her or telling her she was cruel, a person who wanted nothing had felt like water.

That night, in her room, with the house quiet and Claire finally asleep or pretending to be across the hall, Brooke did the thing she had been building toward all week without admitting it. She recorded a short video. No footage, no editing, just her own face lit by the monitor, tired, plain, done with being careful.

"I'm going to ask something and I don't care if it makes me sound crazy," she said. "The people who got sick and got better. Are they different now? Not sad-different. Not tired-different. Different. Do they need to be near you all the time. Do they eat things they used to hate. Do animals not like them anymore." She stopped. She had a list open off-camera and she did not read from it, but she knew it by heart now. "I'm not saying I know what it is. I don't. I'm asking if anyone else is seeing it, because everyone around me is telling me I'm imagining it, and I need to know if I am."

She titled it *Is anyone else seeing this?* and posted it under her own name and closed the laptop and lay down and did not expect anything, because the last three videos had gotten mockery and a joke about fruit punch.

When she woke in the night and checked, out of the habit she could not break, there were comments.

*my mom too. exactly this. i thought it was just me.*

*our dog won't go near my brother anymore and he grew up with him. what is happening.*

Brooke lay in the dark holding the phone above her face, its light the only light in the room, as new replies arrived beneath the first two. She was not alone. That should have helped. Instead, every reply became another house like hers with something quiet walking through it, all of them finding one another in the dark.
